## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
1. **Identify** key joints (neck, shoulders, elbows, hips, knees, spine) and explain how movement happens at joints, not in the middle of bones.
2. **Construct** a figure drawing from the inside out using the 4-step method (skeleton → simple shapes → muscle masses → skin).
3. **Apply** the spine S-curve and muscle pair principle (contract = bulge, relax = flatten) to make character drawings feel alive instead of stiff.

## Key Inquiry Question

> What's underneath a **green sea turtle's (Kasa)** shell that makes it move — and how does understanding the skeleton and muscles beneath the surface make our animated characters feel alive?

## Local Context: Kenyan Ocean Animal

**Animal:** Green Sea Turtle (*Chelonia mydas*) — **Kasa** in Swahili  
**Habitat:** Seagrass beds in Watamu Marine National Park, Diani-Chale Marine Reserve.  
**Why this animal?** A sea turtle's anatomy is a perfect teaching tool: the shell is a rigid ribcage (you can see how the skeleton defines the shape), the flippers have clear joints (shoulder, elbow, wrist), and the spine connects head to shell. When a turtle swims, you can SEE the muscle groups working — the shoulder rotates, the flipper bends at the elbow, the spine flexes slightly with each stroke.  
**Conservation note:** Green sea turtles are Endangered. They mistake plastic bags for jellyfish (their food). KWS and Local Ocean Trust run nesting protection at Watamu — community volunteers patrol beaches at night during nesting season (March-June).

## Lesson Development

### Introduction (10 min)

**Hook:** Show a photo of a sea turtle swimming. Ask: *"What's inside that shell? How does it move?"*

Explain: *A turtle shell is actually its ribcage — the bones grew together into armor. The flippers have the same joints as your arm: shoulder, elbow, wrist. When you understand what's UNDERNEATH, you can animate anything believably.*

Have learners stand up and do a slow motion walk. Ask them to notice:
- Which joints bend? (hips, knees, ankles, shoulders, elbows)
- What happens to muscles when they bend? (one side bulges, other side stretches)
- Does the spine bend? (yes — it's an S-curve, not straight)

### Step 1: The Skeleton — Your Movement Map (15 min)

Teach key joints using the turtle-to-human comparison:

```
Human Skeleton:                    Sea Turtle Skeleton:
┌─────────┐                        ┌─────────┐
│  Head   │ ← neck joint           │  Head   │ ← neck joint
├─────────┤                        ├─────────┤
│ Ribcage │ ← fairly rigid         │ SHELL   │ ← ribcage fused into shell
│  Spine  │ ← S-curve, flexible    │  Spine  │ ← connects head to shell
├────┬────┤                        ├───┬───┤
│Hip │Hip │ ← ball joints          │Hip│Hip│ ← ball joints
│    │    │                        │   │   │
│Knee│Knee│ ← hinge (one way)      │Elb│Elb│ ← hinge joints
│    │    │                        │   │   │
│Ankl│Ankl│ ← flex/rotate           │Wrist│  ← flipper joints
└────┴────┘                        └───┴───┘
```

**Key rules:**
1. Movement happens at JOINTS, not in the middle of bones
2. Hinge joints (elbow, knee) only bend ONE way
3. Ball joints (shoulder, hip) rotate in all directions
4. The spine is an S-curve — bending the torso means bending the WHOLE spine, not just the waist

### Step 2: Construction Drawing — Inside Out (20 min)

Teach the 4-step construction method using a dolphin:

**Step 1: Simplified skeleton**
Draw a stick figure with joint circles:
```
  O     ← Head
  |     ← Neck
  ⊕     ← Shoulder joint
  |     ← Spine (slight S-curve)
  ⊕     ← Hip joint
  |     ← Tail
```

**Step 2: Add simple 3D shapes**
- Head → sphere
- Torso → cylinder (slightly tapered)
- Tail → cone
- Flippers → flattened cylinders

**Step 3: Add muscle masses**
- Dolphin body is mostly muscle — smooth, streamlined bulges
- Where the body bends, muscles compress (slight bulge on inside of curve)
- The tail has visible muscle bands (peduncle — the powerful swimming muscles)

**Step 4: Smooth skin over everything**
- Draw smooth contours over the muscle shapes
- The result should look like a real dolphin, not a stick figure

### Step 3: Gesture Drawing Practice (20 min)

**30-second gesture drawing exercise:**

Show reference photos of ocean animals in motion (turtle swimming, dolphin jumping, octopus crawling). Learners draw the WHOLE figure in 30 seconds — no details, just the LINE OF ACTION (the main flow of the pose).

**Rules:**
- Don't draw details (eyes, scales, patterns)
- Draw the MOVEMENT, not the shape
- Find the line of action — the main curve that runs through the body
- Use loose, flowing lines (not careful, tight lines)

Do 8 gestures (4 minutes total):
1. Turtle swimming (30 sec)
2. Turtle from different angle (30 sec)
3. Dolphin mid-jump (30 sec)
4. Dolphin swimming (30 sec)
5. Octopus reaching with tentacles (30 sec)
6. Octopus jet propulsion (30 sec)
7. Crab walking sideways (30 sec)
8. Free choice ocean animal (30 sec)

### Step 4: Sharing & Feedback (15 min)

Learners hold up their best gesture drawing. Class discusses:
- *"Can you see the line of action?"*
- *"Does the figure feel like it's moving, even though it's a quick sketch?"*
- *"Which construction step helped the most?"*

|----------|------------------------|-------------------|------------|--------------|
| Joint Knowledge | Cannot identify where joints are | Identifies major joints but not their type | Knows joint types (hinge vs ball) and movement limits | Knows all joints + how they connect + spine S-curve |
| Construction | Draws surface only (no structure) | Attempts skeleton but proportions wrong | 4-step construction with correct proportions | 4-step construction + muscle masses + correct proportions |
| Gesture Drawing | Stiff, detailed, incomplete | Somewhat loose but no line of action | Clear line of action; loose, flowing | Dynamic line of action + weight + energy in 30 seconds |

## Extended Activity

**Anatomy Sketchbook:** Start a dedicated sketchbook. Each week, draw one Kenyan ocean animal using the 4-step construction method. After 8 weeks, you'll have 8 anatomically-understood animals ready to animate. Start with: turtle (week 1), dolphin (week 2), octopus (week 3), clownfish (week 4)...

## Differentiation

| For learners who need more support | For advanced learners |
|-------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Provide pre-drawn skeleton templates to trace over | Study Bodies in Motion (bodiesinmotion.photo) — high-res photo sequences at 14-16fps. Draw 5 frames from a sequence. |
| Focus on 60-second gestures instead of 30 seconds | Add muscle anatomy: name the major muscle groups (deltoid, pectoral, quadricep) and show how they bulge/flatten during movement |
| Use only the dolphin (simplest shape) | Compare human anatomy to ocean animal anatomy — find homologous structures (flipper = arm, tail = spine extension) |
