## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
1. Explain how background design establishes mood, depth, and setting in an animation.
2. Create a 3-layer background (foreground, midground, background) using 1-point or 2-point perspective and atmosphere perspective (distant objects lighter and bluer).
3. Design a coral reef environment for a Kenyan ocean animal character that supports the scene's mood and staging.

## Key Inquiry Question

> A clownfish lives inside its anemone — the anemone is its home, its shelter,
> and its world. How do we design the "anemone" for our animation characters —
> the background world that makes them feel like they truly belong?

## Local Context: Kenyan Ocean Animal

**Animal:** Clownfish (*Samaki Dart* — the name we use for our brave little clownfish character)  
**Habitat:** Coral reefs of Malindi Marine National Park, Mombasa Marine National Park, and Watamu Marine National Park. Clownfish live in a symbiotic relationship with sea anemones — the anemone's stinging tentacles protect the clownfish, and the clownfish brings food and cleans the anemone.  
**Why this animal?** The clownfish is the perfect character for a background design lesson because **it cannot exist without its environment.** A clownfish without its anemone is like a character floating in a void — it looks wrong, it feels wrong, it IS wrong. The anemone IS the background. The coral reef IS the world. This lesson teaches learners that backgrounds aren't decoration — they're where the character lives, breathes, and belongs. We'll design a coral reef environment that makes our clownfish feel at home.  
**Conservation note:** Clownfish themselves are not endangered, but their **coral reef habitats are severely threatened**. Coral bleaching from rising ocean temperatures, destructive fishing practices, and anchor damage from boats all threaten Kenya's reef ecosystems. KWS manages marine protected areas (MPAs) at Watamu, Malindi, and Mombasa to protect coral reefs. Local organizations like Coral Reef Conservation (REEF) and the Wildlife Conservation Society (WCS) run reef restoration projects — growing new coral on underwater structures. When you design your reef background, you're also designing awareness: a beautiful reef makes people want to protect it.

## Lesson Development

### Introduction (5-10 min)

Show photos of a clownfish in its anemone on a Kenyan coral reef (Malindi or Watamu Marine National Park). Ask learners:

- *"Where does the clownfish live? Can it survive without the anemone?"* (Answer: no — the anemone is its shelter)
- *"If I animated the clownfish on a plain white background, would it feel real?"* (Answer: no — it looks like it's floating in nothing)
- *"What makes the reef feel like a real place?"* (Answer: depth — things close up, things far away; color; detail; light)

Connect to the lesson: **Audiences spend 50% of their time looking at the BACKGROUND, not the character.** A great background makes your animation feel real. A bad one makes it feel flat. Today we learn how to build the "anemone" for our characters — the world they inhabit.

### Step 1: Concept Introduction — Perspective, Depth & Mood (15 min)

**Why backgrounds matter:**
- Establishes WHERE and WHEN the story happens
- Sets the MOOD before the character even appears
- Gives the character somewhere to EXIST (no floating in void!)
- Creates DEPTH (foreground, midground, background)
- Supports the character's staging (focal point)

**Perspective — The Foundation:**

```
PERSPECTIVE TYPES BY AGE:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

1-POINT PERSPECTIVE (Ages 10-12)
  Everything vanishes to ONE point on the horizon.
  Use for: hallways, roads, rooms, simple reef scenes

       ●  ← vanishing point
      ╱│╲
     ╱ │ ╲
    ╱  │  ╲   All lines converge to ONE point
   ╱   │   ╲
  ─────┼─────  ← horizon line

2-POINT PERSPECTIVE (Ages 12-14)
  Two vanishing points on the horizon.
  Use for: buildings, coral formations seen from corner

  ●─────────────────────●  ← two vanishing points
   ╲                   ╱
    ╲                 ╱
     ╲      │|       ╱   Vertical edges stay vertical
      ╲     │|      ╱
       ╲    │|     ╱

ATMOSPHERE PERSPECTIVE (All Ages)
  Things farther away look:
  - LIGHTER (closer to sky/water color)
  - BLUER (atmosphere scatters blue light)
  - LESS DETAILED (can't see fine details far away)
  - LOWER CONTRAST (darks get lighter, lights get darker)

  This is FREE DEPTH — just by making distant things
  lighter and bluer. No math required!
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**The 3-Layer Depth System:**

```
EVERY BACKGROUND SHOULD HAVE 3 LAYERS:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

  FOREGROUND        MIDGROUND         BACKGROUND
  (closest)         (action zone)     (farthest)
  ┌──────┐         ┌──────┐         ┌──────┐
  │Most  │         │Medium│         │Least │
  │detail│         │detail│         │detail│
  │Darkest│        │Where │         │Lightest│
  │Biggest│        │char. │         │Smallest│
  │      │         │stands│         │      │
  │Can be│         │      │         │Sky/  │
  │silhou│         │      │         │distant│
  │ette  │         │      │         │reef  │
  └──────┘         └──────┘         └──────┘

  Example for coral reef:
  FOREGROUND:  A coral branch or seaweed in the corner
               (dark, detailed, frames the shot)
  MIDGROUND:   The anemone where the clownfish lives
               (medium detail, where action happens)
  BACKGROUND:  Distant reef, lighter blue water, sun
               rays filtering down (light, hazy)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**Mood through environment:**

| Mood | Colors | Shapes | Lighting |
|------|--------|--------|----------|
| Safe/happy (reef home) | Warm, bright, saturated | Rounded, organic | Bright, even |
| Mysterious (deep reef) | Dark blue, teal | Flowing, hidden | Low, dappled |
| Dangerous (storm) | Dark, desaturated | Sharp angles, jagged | Harsh contrast |
| Peaceful (morning reef) | Soft warm blue, golden | Gentle curves | Soft glow from above |

### Step 2: Guided Practice — Build a Coral Reef Background in Scratch (15 min)

Walk through creating a 3-layer coral reef background in Scratch's backdrop editor. Demonstrate on the projector; learners follow along.

**Step-by-step in Scratch Backdrop Editor:**

```
SCRATCH BACKDROP: CORAL REEF FOR CLOWNFISH
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. OPEN THE BACKDROP EDITOR
   - In Scratch, click "Choose a Backdrop" → "Paint"
   - OR click the "Backdrops" tab at the top

2. DRAW THE BACKGROUND LAYER (farthest)
   - Select the Rectangle tool
   - Fill color: light blue (#87CEEB — sky/water blue)
   - Draw a rectangle covering the TOP 2/3 of the canvas
   - This is the "deep water" — light and hazy
   - Select the Line tool, draw a few horizontal wavy lines
     in slightly lighter blue — these are distant water
     currents (LOW detail, LOW contrast)

3. DRAW THE MIDGROUND LAYER (where the clownfish lives)
   - Select the Ellipse tool
   - Fill color: green (#2E8B57 — sea anemone green)
   - Draw a cluster of overlapping circles in the
     center-bottom area — this is the ANEMONE
   - Add tentacle lines radiating outward (Line tool,
     thin, slightly lighter green)
   - Add a few coral shapes (branching, like tree branches)
     in orange/pink around the anemone
   - This is MEDIUM detail — enough to recognize but
     not overly complex

4. DRAW THE FOREGROUND LAYER (closest)
   - Select the Brush tool
   - Color: dark teal (#006400 — darkest green)
   - Draw a coral branch or seaweed strand in the
     BOTTOM-LEFT corner
   - This FRAMES the shot — like a doorframe
   - Keep it as a rough silhouette (dark shape,
     not much internal detail)
   - This is the FOREGROUND — darkest, biggest, frames
     the viewer's eye toward the center

5. ADD LIGHT
   - Select the Brush tool, low thickness
   - Color: pale yellow with low opacity (if Scratch
     supports it, or just a very light yellow)
   - Draw 2-3 diagonal lines from the TOP-RIGHT going
     down-left — these are SUN RAYS filtering through
     the water
   - Light comes from above-right → shadows fall
     to the bottom-left

6. CHECK YOUR LAYERS:
   - Background: light blue water (farthest, lightest)
   - Midground: anemone + coral (where clownfish lives)
   - Foreground: dark coral branch in corner (frames shot)
   - Sun rays from top-right (light source)
   ✓ Your clownfish now has a HOME!
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**Scratch code to place your clownfish IN the background:**

```scratch
When [green flag] clicked
  // Place clownfish in its anemone (midground)
  go to x: 0, y: -40
  set size to 60%  // smaller = feels deeper in scene

  // Hovering motion (clownfish dart and hover)
  forever
    // Small dart to the right
    glide 0.3 secs to x: 20, y: -40
    wait 0.1 seconds
    // Dart back to anemone
    glide 0.4 secs to x: 0, y: -40
    // Hover — gentle up-down
    repeat 10
      change y by 2
      wait 0.05 seconds
    end
    repeat 10
      change y by -2
      wait 0.05 seconds
    end
  end
```

> **Teacher note:** The clownfish should stay in the MIDGROUND area of the
> background — where the anemone is. This teaches learners that the character
> has a "spot" in the scene. The foreground frames them; the background gives
> depth behind them.

### Step 3: Independent Practice — Design Your Own Ocean Environment (15 min)

Learners create their own background for a Kenyan ocean animal character.

**Task: Design a 3-layer background for your chosen animal**

Choose one animal and design its home environment:

| Animal | Environment to Design | Mood |
|--------|----------------------|------|
| Clownfish | Coral reef with anemone | Safe, colorful, bright |
| Green sea turtle (*kasa*) | Seagrass bed with coral caves | Peaceful, gentle |
| Octopus (*pweza*) | Rocky reef crevice with hiding spots | Mysterious, textured |
| Whale shark (*papa shinga*) | Open ocean with distant reef | Vast, awe-inspiring |
| Dolphin (*pomboo*) | Surface water with horizon and sky | Playful, energetic |

**Requirements:**
1. **3 layers** — foreground (dark, frames shot), midground (where animal stands), background (light, distant)
2. **Atmosphere perspective** — background layer is lighter and bluer than foreground
3. **One light source** — rays or brightness coming from one direction
4. **Mood** — the background should FEEL a specific way (safe, mysterious, vast, etc.)

**For Krita users (intermediate):**

```
KRITA BACKGROUND SETUP — LAYERED REEF
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. CREATE LAYERS (Layer → New Layer)
   - Layer 1: "Background" (bottom)
   - Layer 2: "Midground" (middle)
   - Layer 3: "Foreground" (top)
   - Layer 4: "Character" (for the animal, animated later)

2. ENABLE PERSPECTIVE GRID
   - Settings → Dockers → Perspective Grid
   - Set up 1-point or 2-point perspective
   - Use the grid as a guide for reef structures

3. PAINT BACKGROUND LAYER
   - Large soft brush, light blue
   - Gradient from lighter at top (surface light) to
     darker at bottom (deep water)
   - Very few details — distant reef is just shapes

4. PAINT MIDGROUND LAYER
   - Medium brush, more detail
   - Anemone, coral formations, seagrass
   - Medium brightness — between foreground and background

5. PAINT FOREGROUND LAYER
   - Smaller brush, most detail
   - Coral branch or seaweed in the corner
   - Darkest colors — frames the shot

6. ADD LIGHT
   - New layer above all: "Light Rays"
   - Soft brush, low opacity, pale yellow
   - Diagonal strokes from top-right
   - Set layer blend mode to "Screen" or "Add"
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

> **Teacher note:** For learners using paper and colored pencils (low-resource
> alternative), use the printed perspective worksheet. Sketch the 3 layers in
> pencil first, then add color. The same principles apply — foreground dark,
> background light and blue, one light source.

---

### Step 4: Sharing & Feedback (10 min)

Learners display their backgrounds (on screen or hold up paper sketches). Class does a "gallery walk" or pair-share feedback.

**Feedback questions for each background:**
- *"Can you see the 3 layers? Point to the foreground, midground, and background."*
- *"Does the background make you feel the intended mood? What mood do you get?"*
- *"Where would the character stand? Is there a clear 'spot' for them?"*
- *"Does the atmosphere perspective work — are distant things lighter and bluer?"*

**Closing discussion:**
- *"Before this lesson, did you think backgrounds mattered? Has your mind changed?"*
- *"Which background felt the most 'real' or 'lived-in'? Why?"*
- *"How would you redesign your background if your animal was in danger (storm, pollution)? What would change?"*

Connect to conservation: *"When you design a beautiful coral reef, you make people care about reefs. A stunning background isn't just art — it's advocacy. People protect what they love, and they love what they see."*

## Assessment

| Criteria | BE (Below Expectation) | AE (Approaching) | ME (Meets) | EE (Exceeds) |
|----------|------------------------|-------------------|------------|--------------|
| Background concept (3 layers, perspective) | Cannot identify foreground, midground, or background | Identifies some layers but confuses them; no perspective used | Creates a background with clear 3-layer depth (foreground, midground, background) using basic perspective | Creates a 3-layer background with correct perspective AND atmosphere perspective (distant objects lighter and bluer) |
| Mood & environment design | Background is flat or random; no mood or theme | Background has some theme but mood is unclear or inconsistent | Background establishes a clear mood through color, shape, and lighting that matches the animal's habitat | Background establishes a strong mood through deliberate color palette, shape language, and lighting; mood is immediately recognizable |
| Character integration | Character floats on background with no connection | Character is placed on background but scale or position feels off | Character is placed in the midground "spot" at appropriate scale; background supports staging | Character is integrated naturally — scale, position, and lighting match the background; the animal looks like it BELONGS there |

## Extended Activity

**Homework: Before & After Reef**

1. Design TWO versions of the same coral reef background:
   - **Version A: "Healthy Reef"** — vibrant colors, diverse coral, clear water, clownfish in a thriving anemone. Mood: safe, alive, beautiful.
   - **Version B: "Bleached Reef"** — pale/white coral, fewer colors, murky water, empty anemone. Mood: sad, damaged, urgent.

2. Save both as Scratch backdrops or Krita images.

3. Bring both to the next class. We'll use them in a 2-shot animation: a healthy reef that becomes bleached — a conservation story told through background design alone, with no words.

**This teaches:** Backgrounds aren't just settings — they can tell stories and deliver messages. A changing background IS a narrative.

**Conservation connection:** Research one fact about coral bleaching in Kenya's marine parks from KWS (kws.go.ke). Write it as a title card to display between your two backgrounds. Example: *"Rising ocean temperatures cause coral bleaching. Watamu Marine National Park lost 30% of its coral in the 2016 bleaching event. Reef restoration projects are rebuilding — but it takes years."*

## Differentiation

| For learners who need more support | For advanced learners |
|-------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Use 1-point perspective only; provide a pre-drawn grid worksheet to trace over | Use 2-point or 3-point perspective; challenge them to draw a reef scene from an unusual angle (looking up from the ocean floor) |
| Limit to 2 layers (background + midground); skip foreground | Add a 4th layer (deep foreground — particles/bubbles floating closest to camera) for extra depth |
| Use Scratch backdrop editor only (simpler tools, no layers to manage) | Use Krita with full layer system, perspective grid docker, and brush textures for realistic coral |
| Provide a coloring-page-style template of a reef with layer areas labeled | Challenge them to design a FANTASY reef environment — impossible coral shapes, bioluminescent anemones — while keeping correct perspective and lighting |
| Allow paper and colored pencils as the medium (same concepts, no software needed) | Have them create 3 different backdrops for the SAME animal showing 3 different moods (safe, dangerous, peaceful) and present which is most effective |
