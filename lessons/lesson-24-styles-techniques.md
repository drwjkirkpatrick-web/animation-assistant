## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
1. Describe at least 4 different animation styles (frame-by-frame, tweening/puppet, stop motion, pixel art) and explain what makes each unique.
2. Create a short animation of a clownfish in at least 2 different styles using appropriate tools for each.
3. Appreciate that there is no single "right" way to animate — finding the style that excites you is the key to keeping animating — and value Kenya's reef biodiversity as creative inspiration.

## Key Inquiry Question

> There are many ways to bring **Samaki Dart the Clownfish** to life — from hand-drawing every frame to moving paper cut-outs to placing pixels one by one. Which animation style makes YOU excited to create?

## Local Context: Kenyan Ocean Animal

**Animal:** Clownfish (Anemonefish) — *Samaki Dart* (character name from reference)  
**Habitat:** Coral reefs in Mombasa Marine National Park, Malindi Marine National Park, and Watamu — Kenya's Indian Ocean coast. Clownfish live in sea anemones on the reef, darting out to feed and retreating for safety.  
**Why this animal?** The clownfish is the **perfect first animation character** — it has a simple oval body, bright memorable colors (orange with white stripes), and a distinctive movement pattern (darting quickly, then hovering). Its simple shape works beautifully across ALL animation styles: it's easy to draw frame-by-frame, simple to rig as a cut-out puppet, recognizable even as a tiny pixel art sprite, and can be made from paper or clay for stop motion. This makes it the ideal character for a lesson about exploring different styles — **same character, different techniques, different results.**  
**Conservation note:** Clownfish depend on **healthy coral reefs** (*mwamba wa makorali*), which are threatened in Kenya by coral bleaching (caused by rising ocean temperatures), overfishing, and destructive fishing practices. The Kenya Wildlife Service (KWS) supports **reef restoration projects** — growing coral fragments in nurseries and transplanting them to damaged reefs. **Class discussion:** When you animate a clownfish, you're animating an animal that needs healthy reefs to survive. Sharing your clownfish animation can help people care about protecting Kenya's coral reefs.

## Lesson Development

### Introduction (10 min)

**Hook:** Show the SAME clownfish character animated in 3 different styles, back to back:

1. **Frame-by-frame:** A hand-drawn clownfish darting with squash & stretch (2 seconds)
2. **Stop motion:** A paper cut-out clownfish wobbling across a coral background (2 seconds)
3. **Pixel art:** A tiny 16×16 pixel clownfish swimming in a retro game style (2 seconds)

Ask learners: **"Same fish, same movement — but they all look completely different. Why?"**

Guide them to understand: **The STYLE changes how the animation feels, even when the subject is the same.** Animation isn't one technique — it's a whole toolbox of different approaches, and finding the one that excites you is the key to keeping you animating.

**Introduce the Styles Menu (display on board):**

| Style | How It Works | Vibe | Best For |
|-------|-------------|------|----------|
| Frame-by-Frame | Draw every single frame by hand | Organic, alive, expressive | Emotion, squash & stretch |
| Tweening / Puppet | Draw once, move parts with software | Smooth, efficient | Long animations, walk/swim cycles |
| Stop Motion | Move physical objects, photograph each frame | Tactile, real, charming | Hands-on learners, crafts |
| Cut-Out | Move separate paper/digital pieces | Crafty, fast, charming | Quick productions, younger kids |
| Pixel Art | Draw pixel-by-pixel, retro game style | Retro, nostalgic, precise | Game lovers, limited palettes |
| 3D Animation | Move 3D models with keyframes/rigs | Polished, cinematic | World-building, movie look |

**Today's mission:** Learners will try **at least 2 styles** with the same clownfish character and discover which one excites them most.

### Step 1: Concept Introduction — Style Deep Dives (15 min)

Teach the top 4 styles using Samaki Dart the Clownfish as the example for each.

**Style 1: Frame-by-Frame / Hand-Drawn**
- **What it is:** Drawing every single frame by hand. The oldest method.
- **Vibe:** Organic, expressive, alive. Every frame is unique.
- **Tools:** Krita, Pencil2D, OpenToonz
- **Clownfish example:** Draw the clownfish darting forward — body compresses (squash) on the turn, stretches on the dart forward. Only frame-by-frame lets you change the shape every frame.
- **Famous examples:** Studio Ghibli films, classic Disney, Cuphead

**Style 2: Tweening / Puppet Animation**
- **What it is:** Draw the character once, then move/rotate the parts using software "tweens."
- **Vibe:** Smooth, consistent, efficient.
- **Tools:** Scratch, Synfig, OpenToonz, Blender
- **Clownfish example:** Draw the clownfish body, tail, and fins as separate pieces. Rotate the tail back and forth — the software fills in the in-between frames automatically.
- **Famous examples:** Most TV cartoons, explainer videos

**Style 3: Stop Motion**
- **What it is:** Moving physical objects and photographing each frame.
- **Vibe:** Tactile, real, charming. You can touch the animation.
- **Tools:** Stop Motion Studio (phone), Raspberry Pi + Pi Camera + Python, Dragonframe (pro)
- **Clownfish example:** Cut a clownfish from paper, place it on a coral background, move it slightly, take a photo, repeat. The paper fish appears to swim!
- **Famous examples:** Wallace & Gromit, Coraline, Kubo, LEGO movies

**Style 4: Pixel Art Animation**
- **What it is:** Drawing small pixel-by-pixel frames, like retro games.
- **Vibe:** Retro, game-like, charming, nostalgic.
- **Tools:** Piskel (free, web), Aseprite (paid), LibreSprite (free), Krita (pixel brush)
- **Clownfish example:** Draw a 16×16 pixel clownfish — just 16 pixels wide! Animate the tail wagging by shifting a few pixels per frame.
- **Famous examples:** Celeste, Undertale, Stardew Valley, classic NES/SNES games

**Code Block — Scratch Cut-Out/Tween Animation of Clownfish:**

```scratch
// === SAMAKI DART THE CLOWNFISH — TWEENING IN SCRATCH ===
// Style: Cut-out / Puppet Animation
// The clownfish is drawn once, then we move it with code.

// Step 1: Draw a clownfish sprite (orange oval body, 
//          white stripes, black-tipped fins)
// Step 2: Set the costume center to the middle of the body

// --- SWIMMING ACROSS THE SCREEN WITH TAIL WAG ---

when green flag clicked
set size to 60 %            // small clownfish
go to x: -200 y: 0          // start at left edge
point in direction 90       // facing right
forever
  // Swim forward smoothly (tween = software fills in the motion)
  glide 3 secs to x: 200 y: 0
  // Turn around
  turn to direction -90     // face left
  // Swim back
  glide 3 secs to x: -200 y: 0
  turn to direction 90      // face right again
end

// --- DARTING MOTION (quick burst) ---
// Press spacebar to make the clownfish dart forward

when [space] key pressed
// Anticipation: pull back slightly first
glide 0.1 secs to x: (x position - 10) y: (y position)
// DART: quick burst forward
glide 0.15 secs to x: (x position + 80) y: (y position)
// The glide blocks = "tweening" — Scratch calculates
// the in-between positions automatically!

// --- TAIL WAG (separate sprite or costume change) ---
// Create 3 costumes: tail-left, tail-center, tail-right
// Switch costumes to animate the tail wagging:

when green flag clicked
forever
  switch costume to [tail-right]
  wait 0.1 seconds
  switch costume to [tail-center]
  wait 0.1 seconds
  switch costume to [tail-left]
  wait 0.1 seconds
  switch costume to [tail-center]
  wait 0.1 seconds
end
```

### Step 2: Guided Practice — Try Style #1 Together (15 min)

The class tries **one style together** as a warm-up. Choose based on available tools:

**Option A: Scratch Cut-Out Animation (if computers available)**

Walk through the code from Step 1 together:
1. Open Scratch (scratch.mit.edu). Create a new project.
2. Draw a simple clownfish: orange oval, 2-3 white vertical stripes, a triangle tail.
3. Set costume center to the middle of the body.
4. Add the swimming code (glide blocks) from the code block above.
5. Add the tail wag (costume switching).
6. Press the green flag — the clownfish swims across the screen with a wagging tail!

**Option B: Paper Cut-Out Stop Motion (low-resource, if phones available)**

```text
=== PAPER CUT-OUT CLOWNFISH STOP MOTION ===

MATERIALS:
- Orange paper (clownfish body)
- White paper (stripes — cut thin strips and glue on)
- Blue paper or cloth (ocean background)
- Green paper (coral/seaweed — optional)
- Phone with Stop Motion Studio app (free)
  OR: Raspberry Pi + Pi Camera with Python script (below)

STEPS:
1. Cut out a simple clownfish shape from orange paper
2. Glue 2-3 white paper stripes across the body
3. Cut out a separate tail piece, attach with a brad fastener
   (so the tail can move independently)
4. Set up your ocean background on a table
5. Open Stop Motion Studio (or your camera app)
6. Place the clownfish at the left edge → take photo (Frame 1)
7. Move the fish 1 cm to the right → take photo (Frame 2)
8. Move the fish 1 cm right + wag tail slightly → photo (Frame 3)
9. Continue: move + wag, move + wag, move + wag...
10. Take 24-30 frames total
11. Play back at 12 FPS → your paper clownfish swims!

TIPS:
- Keep the camera STILL (use a stand or prop the phone)
- Move the fish the SAME amount each frame for smooth motion
- Smaller movements = slower, smoother swim
- Larger movements = faster, choppier (good for darting!)
```

**Raspberry Pi alternative (for low-resource settings with Pi):**

```python
# === CLOWNFISH STOP MOTION WITH RASPBERRY PI ===
# Uses Pi Camera to capture frames for stop motion animation

import os
import time
from picamera import PiCamera

# Set up camera
camera = PiCamera()
camera.resolution = (640, 480)
camera.rotation = 180  # adjust if image is upside down

# Create output folder
output_dir = "/home/pi/clownfish-stopmotion"
os.makedirs(output_dir, exist_ok=True)

frame_count = 0
print("=== CLOWNFISH STOP MOTION CAPTURE ===")
print("Press ENTER to capture a frame.")
print("Type 'done' to finish.")
print("Move the paper clownfish slightly before each frame!\n")

while True:
    command = input(f"Frame {frame_count:03d} — Press ENTER to capture (or type 'done'): ")
    if command.lower() == 'done':
        break
    
    filename = os.path.join(output_dir, f"frame_{frame_count:04d}.jpg")
    camera.capture(filename)
    print(f"  ✓ Saved {filename}")
    frame_count += 1

camera.close()
print(f"\nCaptured {frame_count} frames!")
print(f"Frames saved in: {output_dir}")
print("To play back, use ffmpeg to create a video:")
print(f"  ffmpeg -framerate 12 -i {output_dir}/frame_%04d.jpg "
      f"-c:v libx264 -pix_fmt yuv420p clownfish.mp4")
```

**Style 2 options for independent practice:**

**Option A: Pixel Art in Piskel (web-based, great for ages 10-14)**

```text
=== CLOWNFISH PIXEL ART IN PISKEL ===

1. Go to piskel.cc (or piskel-app.com)
2. Click "Create Sprite"
3. Set canvas size to 32 x 32 (retro game size)
4. Pick colors:
   - Orange: #FF6600 (body)
   - White: #FFFFFF (stripes)
   - Black: #000000 (outline, fin tips)
   - Light blue: #88CCFF (water background — optional)

5. Frame 1: Draw the clownfish facing right
   - Body: orange oval (about 16 pixels wide, 8 tall)
   - Stripes: 2 vertical white lines
   - Tail: black-edged triangle on the left
   - Eye: 1 black pixel

6. Create Frame 2 (click "Duplicate Frame" button):
   - Move the tail pixels 1-2 positions (wag right)
   - Keep the body the same

7. Create Frame 3: 
   - Move tail back to center
   - Move the whole fish 2 pixels right

8. Create Frame 4:
   - Move tail to wag left
   - Move fish 2 more pixels right

9. Set FPS to 8 (bottom of screen)
10. Press Play → your pixel clownfish swims with a wagging tail!

TIPS:
- Keep it SIMPLE — 32x32 means every pixel counts
- Use fewer colors (4-5 max) for authentic retro look
- The tail wag is the key animation — body stays mostly still
```

**Option B: Frame-by-Frame in Krita (for ages 12-17)**

```text
=== CLOWNFISH FRAME-BY-FRAME IN KRITA ===

1. Open Krita → New Document → 640 x 480, 12 FPS
2. Enable Animation: Settings → Dockers → check "Animation" and "Timeline"
3. Frame 1: Draw a clownfish darting right (body stretched, tail back)
4. Frame 2: Draw the clownfish compressed (squash — body rounder, 
   fins splayed) — this is the "turn" moment
5. Frame 3: Draw the clownfish stretched again (darting forward)
6. Frame 4: Draw the clownfish at normal rest (hovering in anemone)
7. Press Play — the clownfish darts and settles!

SQUASH & STRETCH RULE:
- When the fish darts forward: body STRETCHES (longer, thinner)
- When the fish turns: body SQUASHES (shorter, rounder)
- When the fish hovers: body is NORMAL shape
- This shape change is what makes frame-by-frame special!
```

**Option C: Stop Motion with Clay (for hands-on learners)**

```text
=== CLAYMATION CLOWNFISH ===

1. Shape orange clay into a small fish body (oval)
2. Add thin white clay stripes
3. Make a separate clay tail piece
4. Set up on a table with blue cloth background
5. Take photos: move clay fish slightly each frame
6. The clay tail can bend and wag — something paper can't do!
7. 24-30 frames at 12 FPS = 2-3 seconds of animation
```

**Success check:** Each learner has animated the clownfish in 2 different styles and completed their Style Exploration Log, identifying which style they enjoyed more and why.

### Step 4: Sharing & Feedback (10 min)

Learners present their two clownfish animations to the class or in small groups.

**Gallery walk format (if space allows):**
- Learners display their animations on their screens (or play their stop motion videos on their phones).
- Half the class walks around viewing, while the other half presents. Then switch.

**Critique framework:**
- **I notice…** ("I notice your pixel art clownfish has a really smooth tail wag")
- **I wonder…** ("I wonder how the stop motion would look with a coral background?")
- **What if…** ("What if you tried the same darting motion in 3D in Blender?")

**Class discussion:**
- "Which style was the most popular in our class? Why?"
- "Which style was the hardest? Which was the easiest?"
- "Did anyone discover a style they'd never tried before but loved?"
- "Remember: most professional animators mix styles. You don't have to pick just one forever. Try lots, combine what you love."

**Style mixing examples (show on projector):**
- *Spider-Verse* combined 3D animation with 2D hand-drawn effects and comic book halftone patterns
- *Kubo and the Two Strings* combined stop motion with CGI enhancement
- Many YouTube animators combine frame-by-frame characters with motion graphics backgrounds

**Final thought for the class:**
> "You've now tried multiple animation styles with Samaki Dart the Clownfish — a fish that lives right here in Kenya's coral reefs. Whether you love drawing every frame, moving paper cut-outs, or placing pixels, there's a style for YOU. The most important thing is to keep animating — because every style, and every reef, is worth preserving."
