## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
1. Identify safe, age-appropriate online platforms for sharing animation work and explain why each is suitable.
2. Post an animation to the Scratch community with a clear title, context, and a specific feedback question using the Sandwich Method.
3. Demonstrate respectful online behaviour by giving constructive feedback to peers and responding gracefully to feedback received.

## Key Inquiry Question

> How does a dolphin pod stay connected and safe in the open ocean — and how can
> we build the same kind of supportive community when we share our animations
> online?

## Local Context: Kenyan Ocean Animal

**Animal:** Bottlenose Dolphin (*Pomboo*)  
**Habitat:** Kisite-Mpunguti Marine Reserve (Kwale County) and Watamu Marine National Park (Kilifi County). Dolphins live in pods of 10-30 individuals along Kenya's south coast.  
**Why this animal?** Dolphins are one of the most social animals in the ocean. They communicate with clicks and whistles, cooperate to hunt, and support injured pod members. A dolphin pod is the perfect metaphor for an online animation community: individuals who share, help, and protect each other. Just as a dolphin doesn't swim alone, an animator doesn't grow alone — community accelerates learning.  
**Conservation note:** Bottlenose dolphins face threats from fishing bycatch and water pollution along the Kenyan coast. KWS (Kenya Wildlife Service) regulates dolphin-watching tourism at Kisite-Mpunguti to prevent harassment from tourist boats. Speed limits for boats protect dolphins from injury. When we build online communities, we also learn to protect what matters — both marine life and each other.

## Lesson Development

### Introduction (5-10 min)

Show the 1-2 minute video of a bottlenose dolphin pod (*pomboo*) communicating and cooperating at Kisite-Mpunguti Marine Reserve. Ask learners:

- *"What do you notice about how the dolphins interact?"*
- *"Why don't they swim alone?"*
- *"What would happen if one dolphin was separated from the pod?"*

Connect to the lesson: **Scratch is like a dolphin pod.** When you share your animation, you join a community of thousands of young creators. You give feedback (like dolphin clicks — specific signals), receive feedback, and grow together. Today we learn how to be a good "pod member" online.

---

### Step 1: Concept Introduction — Platforms, Posting & Etiquette (15 min)

Teach the three core concepts using the dolphin pod metaphor.

**1. Where to share (by age):**

| Age | Platform | Why |
|-----|----------|-----|
| 10-13 | Scratch (scratch.mit.edu) | Moderated, safe, built for kids. Loves/favorites/remixes. |
| 13-15 | Scratch + YouTube (with comment filters) | Broader audience. Turn on "hold potentially inappropriate comments for review." |
| 15-17 | ArtStation, 11 Second Club, Vimeo | Professional-adjacent communities. |

For this class (12-14), **Scratch is our home pod.**

**2. How to post the right way:**
- **Title it well:** "Dolphin Swim Cycle — feedback on timing welcome!" not "test.sb3"
- **Include context:** What tool? How long did it take? What were you trying to learn?
- **Ask a specific question:** "I think the tail flap is too fast — does it feel natural?" Specific questions get specific answers.
- **Be humble but proud:** "This is my second animation ever. Happy with the arc, working on the timing."

**3. The Sandwich Method for giving feedback:**

```
Sandwich Method:
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│  🍞 TOP: Positive (specific)    │  "The arc on the dolphin's
│                                  │   jump is really smooth!"
├─────────────────────────────────┤
│  🧀 MIDDLE: Constructive         │  "The splash might be too
│       (one thing, actionable)    │   small for a dolphin that
│                                  │   size — try scaling it up."
├─────────────────────────────────┤
│  🍞 BOTTOM: Encouragement        │  "Your timing is really
│                                  │   improving. Keep going!"
└─────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Feedback etiquette rules:**
1. Be specific: "The swim cycle slides" not "it looks weird"
2. One thing at a time: pick the most important issue, not 10 things
3. Suggest, don't command: "What if you tried adding anticipation?" not "you need anticipation"
4. Don't compare to pros: compare to their past work
5. Use the principle vocabulary: name the principle (timing, arcs, follow-through)

**Online safety rules (display on board):**
- Never share your real name, age, school, or location online
- Use a username, not your real name
- Tell a parent/teacher if someone is being mean or asking for personal info
- Your work is yours — don't let anyone claim it as their own

### Step 2: Guided Practice — Share & Comment on Scratch (15 min)

Walk through the process together as a class.

**Part A: Sharing your project on Scratch**

Step-by-step (demonstrate on projector):

```
HOW TO SHARE ON SCRATCH:
1. Open your saved Scratch project (the dolphin swim animation
   from the previous lesson, or the starter project provided).
2. Click the orange "Share" button at the top of the screen.
3. Fill in the project page:
   - Title: "Pomboo the Dolphin — Swim Cycle (feedback welcome!)"
   - Instructions: "Click the green flag. Watch Pomboo swim.
     I used the arc principle for the tail flap."
   - Notes and Credits: "This is my 3rd Scratch animation.
     I'm working on slow-in/slow-out timing. Does the swim
     feel too fast or too slow? Let me know!"
4. Click "Share" to publish.
5. Your project now has a URL you can share with classmates.
```

**Part B: Giving feedback with the Sandwich Method**

Display a classmate's project (or the teacher's demo). Model a Sandwich Method comment together:

```
EXAMPLE SCRATCH COMMENT (Sandwich Method):

🍞 "The timing on the tail flap is really satisfying —
   you can feel the power of each stroke!"

🧀 "One thing: the dolphin's body doesn't rotate slightly
   with each flap. Real dolphins (pomboo) twist a little.
   What if you added a small turn on the Y-axis?"

🍞 "Love that you used a Kenyan dolphin! The arc principle
   is really coming through. Keep going!"
```

Have learners copy this structure onto their printed Sandwich Method cards.

---

### Step 3: Independent Practice — Share, Ask, Comment (15 min)

Learners complete three tasks:

**Task 1: Share your own project on Scratch**
- Open any animation project you've made (dolphin, turtle, fish — any ocean animal)
- Share it with a proper title, context, and ONE specific feedback question
- Copy your project URL into the class chat or shared document

**Task 4: Give Sandwich Method feedback to two classmates**
- Visit two classmates' shared projects (use the URLs from the class document)
- Write one Sandwich Method comment on each, using the printed card as a guide
- Use at least one animation principle word (arcs, timing, squash, follow-through, etc.)

**Task 3: Receive feedback gracefully**
- Check your own project for incoming comments
- Respond to at least one comment: "Thanks for the feedback!" even if you disagree
- If you get a vague comment like "cool," that's fine — not all feedback is useful

**Scratch project — comment-friendly template code:**

Here is a simple Scratch block setup that creates a shareable dolphin swim animation:

```scratch
When [green flag] clicked
  go to x: -200, y: 0
  forever
    // Swim across screen with arc motion
    glide 1.5 secs to x: 200, y: 0
    // Tail flap effect — switch costumes
    switch costume to [dolphin-flap-up]
    wait 0.2 seconds
    switch costume to [dolphin-flap-down]
    wait 0.2 seconds
    // Loop back — reset position
    glide 0.5 secs to x: -200, y: 0
  end
```

> **Teacher note:** Learners who don't have a finished project can use this starter.
> The goal is practising the sharing and feedback process, not the animation quality.

---

### Step 4: Sharing & Feedback (10 min)

Bring the class back together. Ask 3-4 learners to share:

- **What feedback did they receive?** Was it useful? How did it feel?
- **What feedback did they give?** Did they use the Sandwich Method?
- **Did anyone receive a comment that wasn't helpful?** How did they handle it?

Discuss: *"When we give specific, kind feedback, our whole pod gets stronger — just like dolphins that communicate clearly help the whole pod find food and avoid danger."*

Reinforce: feedback is about the WORK, not the PERSON. "The swim cycle slides" doesn't mean "you're bad." It means "the swim cycle has a technical issue to fix."

Celebrate: highlight 2-3 examples of excellent Sandwich Method feedback from the session.

## Assessment

| Criteria | BE (Below Expectation) | AE (Approaching) | ME (Meets) | EE (Exceeds) |
|----------|------------------------|-------------------|------------|--------------|
| Sharing work online | Does not share project or shares with no title/context | Shares project but title or context is vague (e.g., "test") | Shares project with clear title, context, and one specific feedback question | Shares project with excellent title, full context, specific question, and responds to received comments |
| Giving feedback (Sandwich Method) | Gives vague feedback ("looks good") or no feedback | Attempts Sandwich Method but misses a layer or is vague | Gives complete Sandwich Method feedback that is specific and actionable, uses one animation principle term | Gives complete Sandwich Method feedback on multiple projects, uses multiple principle terms, suggests specific solutions |
| Online safety & etiquette | Shares personal info or gives unkind feedback | Knows safety rules but needs reminders to apply them | Follows all safety rules (no personal info, username only, reports mean comments) and gives respectful feedback | Follows safety rules, gives respectful feedback, AND helps peers follow safety rules (models pod behaviour) |

## Extended Activity

**Homework: The Pod Project**

Find one Scratch project by a stranger (not a classmate) that features an animal animation. Leave one Sandwich Method comment on it using at least two animation principle words. Take a screenshot of your comment and bring it to next class.

**Conservation connection:** Research one threat to bottlenose dolphins (*pomboo*) along the Kenyan coast. Write 2-3 sentences about what KWS does to protect them. Share this as a "Notes and Credits" addition to your Scratch project — animation can raise conservation awareness!

## Differentiation

| For learners who need more support | For advanced learners |
|-------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Provide a pre-filled Sandwich Method card with sentence starters ("I like ___", "One thing to try is ___", "Keep ___!") | Challenge them to give feedback on 3+ projects and use the full animation principle vocabulary (arcs, timing, squash & stretch, follow-through, staging, anticipation) |
| Pair them with a buddy for the sharing step — share and comment together | Have them write a 50-word "community guidelines" post for the class Scratch studio, establishing rules for respectful feedback |
| Allow them to share with family first (show on phone/laptop at home) before posting publicly | Introduce them to the 11 Second Club forum (with parent permission) as a next-step community beyond Scratch |
