## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
1. Describe the 12 Principles of Animation vocabulary needed to give specific, useful feedback (knowledge)
2. Apply a structured critique framework (positive → one improvement → one question) to review a peer's ocean-animal animation (skill/practical)
3. Value kind, honest feedback as a tool for growth — both giving and receiving it (value/attitude)

## Key Inquiry Question

> How do we tell a friend that their Kasa the sea turtle animation could be better — without making them feel bad?

## Local Context: Kenyan Ocean Animal

**Animal:** Green Sea Turtle — Swahili: *Kasa* (also *Kobe*)  
**Habitat:** Seagrass beds of **Watamu Marine National Park** and **Diani-Chale Marine Reserve**; nests on Kenyan beaches at night  
**Why this animal?** The green sea turtle glides with steady, graceful arcs — its flippers trace wide curves, and its swimming has natural acceleration and deceleration (slow-in/slow-out). This makes Kasa the ideal subject for a critique lesson: when the timing or arcs are off, learners can *see* it, and the 12 Principles give them the words to describe what's wrong kindly.  
**Conservation note:** Green sea turtles are **Endangered** (IUCN). Main threats: plastic pollution (they mistake bags for jellyfish), fishing bycatch, and nest disturbance. **KWS protects nesting sites at Watamu** — rangers guard females coming ashore to lay eggs and relocate nests to safe enclosures. Local conservation group **Local Ocean Conservation** runs a turtle rescue and release program.

## Lesson Development

### Introduction (8 min)

Show a 20-second video of a green sea turtle (kasa) swimming. Then show a *bad* animation of a turtle — one that slides flatly across the screen with stiff flippers (teacher can make this in 2 minutes, or use a prepared example).

Ask: *"Which one looks more real? What's different about them?"*

Introduce the lesson's core idea: **Good animators don't work alone — they get feedback. But feedback has to be kind AND useful.** Today we learn a framework used by real animation studios (and our AI assistant) to review work.

Write the three critique rules on the board:
1. **Always start with something positive** — find the principle they're doing well, even by accident
2. **One improvement at a time** — never more than 2 things to fix; kids shut down otherwise
3. **Use "I wonder…"** — instead of "your walk is wrong," say "I wonder what would happen if you added a tiny pause on the contact frame?"

### Step 1: Concept Introduction — The Critique Framework (12 min)

Teach the four-part critique structure from Prompt 06. Display it on the board:

```
Animation Critique Framework — "Kind & Useful"

1. WHAT'S WORKING  🌟
   - Name 1-2 specific things done well
   - Tie each one to a NAMED principle
   - Example: "Your turtle's flippers curve nicely — that's great ARCS."

2. ONE THING TO TRY NEXT  🎯
   - A single, specific, actionable improvement
   - Tied to a specific principle
   - Phrased as an experiment: "try adding..." NOT "you need to add..."

3. QUESTION FOR YOU  🤔
   - One question that makes the animator think differently
   - Helps them self-diagnose instead of relying on you
   - Example: "What happens to a real turtle's body when it turns?"

4. (Age-calibrate the depth)
   - 10-12: focus on squash & stretch, timing, arcs — keep it playful
   - 12-14: add follow-through, anticipation, slow in/out — more technical
   - 14-17: full critique including staging, appeal, solid drawing
```

Build a quick **Principles Word Wall** on the board/charter with the 6 principles most relevant today: **Timing, Arcs, Slow-in/Slow-out, Anticipation, Follow-through, Squash & Stretch.** One-sentence kid definition next to each.

### Step 2: Guided Practice — Build & Critique Kasa Together (15 min)

First, everyone builds the *same* quick turtle swim in Scratch so there's a shared reference to critique. Walk through it together:

```
Scratch Blocks — Kasa the Turtle Swim (quick reference build)

[Events]   when [green flag] clicked
[Control]  forever
[Motion]     glide [0.5] secs to x:[100] y:[50]      ← arc up-right
[Motion]     glide [0.8] secs to x:[-50] y:[0]       ← long glide left (slow = heavy)
[Motion]     glide [0.4] secs to x:[50] y:[-40]      ← arc down-right
[Looks]      next costume                             ← flipper stroke
[Control]    wait [0.2] seconds
[Control]  end forever
```

Now the teacher **models a critique** of this animation out loud, using the framework:
- **Working:** "The glide times vary — 0.8 s on the long stroke makes Kasa feel heavy. That's good **timing** showing **weight**."
- **Try next:** "I wonder what would happen if you added a tiny glide *upward* before the turn — that's **anticipation**, like a turtle gathering itself before a stroke."
- **Question:** "Watch the video again — do Kasa's flippers keep moving *after* the body changes direction? That's **follow-through**. Is that in your animation?"

### Step 3: Independent Practice — Pair Critique (20 min)

Learners work in **pairs**. Each person modifies the turtle animation to make it their own (change glide times, add a costume, add a turn, make Kasa dive). Then they **swap and critique** each other's work using a Critique Card.

**Critique Card template (print one per pair):**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  CRITIQUE CARD — Kasa Animation             │
│  Reviewer: ___________  Animator: _________  │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  WHAT'S WORKING  🌟                          │
│  Principle I see done well: ___________      │
│  Because: ___________________________        │
│                                             │
│  ONE THING TO TRY NEXT  🎯                   │
│  Principle: ___________                      │
│  "I wonder what would happen if you..."      │
│  ____________________________                │
│                                             │
│  QUESTION FOR YOU  🤔                        │
│  ____________________________                │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Rules for the round:**
- Reviewer fills in the card, then reads it aloud to the animator *kindly*
- Animator may not argue or defend — only say "thank you" and ask one clarifying question
- Then **swap roles** and repeat

**Challenge add-on:** If a pair finishes early, the animator tries the one suggested improvement and the reviewer checks whether it helped.

### Step 4: Sharing & Feedback (10 min)

Two or three pairs volunteer to share their critique *out loud* with the class — not the animation, the **critique itself**. The class evaluates: *"Was that critique kind? Was it useful? Did it name a principle?"*

Celebrate: the best critique isn't the one that finds the most mistakes — it's the one that helps someone improve and feel good about trying.

## Assessment

| Criteria | BE (Below Expectation) | AE (Approaching) | ME (Meets) | EE (Exceeds) |
|----------|------------------------|-------------------|------------|--------------|
| Identifies principles in others' work | Cannot name any principle, gives vague praise ("it's nice") | Names a principle with prompting (e.g., "timing?") | Independently names the correct principle and explains why | Names 2+ principles accurately; links them to specific moments in the animation |
| Gives kind, structured feedback | Critique is harsh, list-like, or purely negative | Uses the card format but feedback is generic | Follows all 4 parts; phrased as "I wonder…"; starts positive | Feedback is specific, kind, AND leads to a visible improvement when tried |
| Receives feedback gracefully | Defends or argues with the reviewer | Listens but cannot apply the suggestion | Listens, says thank you, attempts the suggested change | Applies the change AND asks a thoughtful follow-up question |

## Extended Activity

**Homework — Be the Assistant**
Use the Critique Framework to review **your own** animation from a previous lesson (or this lesson's turtle). Write it out in the 4-part format as if *you* were the animation assistant talking to yourself. Bring it next class — we'll see if your self-critique matches what a partner would say.

**Conservation connection:** Add a "fact card" to Kasa's animation: one sentence about Watamu turtle nesting and one action people can take (e.g., "Say no to plastic bags — turtles mistake them for jellyfish").

## Differentiation

| For learners who need more support | For advanced learners |
|-------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Provide a **word-bank card** with the 6 principles + one-line definitions + sentence starters ("I notice the _____ looks good because…"). Reduce the critique to just 2 parts: What's Working + One Question. | Require the full 4-part critique using **6 principles** (add staging, appeal, solid drawing). Challenge them to critique not just motion but **staging** — "Where is my eye drawn? Is that where the story is?" Have them give a second round of feedback *after* the animator applies the first fix. |
