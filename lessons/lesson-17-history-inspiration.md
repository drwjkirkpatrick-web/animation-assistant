## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
1. Describe at least three milestones in animation history (thaumatrope, flipbook, first character animation, CGI) and place them on a simple timeline.
2. Create a working sticky-note flipbook of a Kenyan ocean animal using the principle of persistence of vision.
3. Value their own work as part of a 150+ year creative tradition and connect a historical technique to their own animation.

## Key Inquiry Question

> How did people make things move 150 years ago — and how is what *you* are doing today part of that same tradition?

## Local Context: Kenyan Ocean Animal

**Animal:** Clownfish (Anemonefish) — *Samaki Dart*  
**Habitat:** Coral reefs of Watamu Marine National Park and Mombasa Marine National Park, living among sea anemones.  
**Why this animal?** The clownfish has a simple oval body and bright colour pattern — the easiest first character to draw repeatedly, exactly the kind of shape early animators used. Its darting motion (tiny hop between anemone tentacles) is a perfect first flipbook action.  
**Conservation note:** Clownfish depend on healthy coral reefs (*mwamba wa makorali*). Kenya's reefs are threatened by bleaching and overfishing. KWS and local groups run reef restoration projects at Watamu. When we draw reef animals, we also learn why we must protect their home.

## Lesson Development

### Introduction (8 min)

Show the picture set in order: thaumatrope → zoetrope → Steamboat Willie → Ghibli still → Spider-Verse. Ask: *"Which of these is the oldest? Which is the newest? What do they ALL have in common?"* Lead learners to the idea that **all animation is many still pictures shown fast**. Hold up a sticky note and say: *"Today you'll make the same kind of animation people made in 1868 — with this."*

### Step 1: Concept Introduction — Persistence of Vision & The History Arc (15 min)

Teach the history arc as a story, not a lecture. Use the "Did You Know? 🤯 → The Story Behind It → Your Connection" rhythm from Prompt 17.

**Key beats (put on the board as a 5-stop timeline):**

| Year | Moment | Why it matters to YOU |
|------|--------|----------------------|
| 1868 | Flipbook invented | You're making one today |
| 1914 | Winsor McCay — *Gertie the Dinosaur*, 10,000 hand drawings | First character animation ever |
| 1928 | Disney — *Steamboat Willie* | First cartoon with synced sound |
| 1995 | Pixar — *Toy Story* | First fully computer-animated film |
| 2018 | *Spider-Verse* | Broke the "rules" on purpose — 12fps, hand-drawn on 3D |

Explain **persistence of vision**: your eyes hold an image for a split second, so 10–24 pictures a second blend into motion.

> **Your Connection:** *"The flipbook you're about to make uses the exact same trick as a 1868 flipbook AND a 2024 Oscar winner. You are part of this tradition."*

### Step 2: Guided Practice — Make a Clownfish Flipbook Together (18 min)

Everyone draws the **same** flipbook step by step so nobody is stuck. Use the clownfish (simple oval + stripes).

**Flipbook plan (12 frames):** A clownfish resting in an anemone, then darting up to a new anemone, then settling. Each frame the fish moves up a little and its tail bends the opposite way (this is real animation — the tail leads/lags the body).

```text
Frame 1:  Clownfish low-left, tail straight, anemone tentacles around it
Frame 2:  Fish 2mm higher, tail bends slightly left
Frame 3:  Fish 4mm higher, tail bends more left
Frame 4:  Fish 6mm higher, tail straight (mid-dart)
Frame 5:  Fish 8mm higher, tail bends right (overshoot)
Frame 6:  Fish 9mm higher, tail straightening, reaching anemone #2
Frame 7:  Fish settles, tail wags right
Frame 8:  Tail wags left (settle wobble)
Frame 9:  Tail wags smaller right
Frame 10: Tail wags smaller left
Frame 11: Fish resting, tail nearly still
Frame 12: Fish resting, tail still — anemone tentacles sway gently
```

**Teacher demo on the board:** draw frames 1, 4, 7, 12 (the key poses) big, then explain that frames in between are "inbetweens" — a whole job at Disney! Learners draw all 12 on sticky notes, one per note, keeping the anemone in the same spot each time so only the fish moves.

**Quick test:** hold the pad with the thumb, flip fast. Does the fish dart up? If it "jumps," a frame is missing or too far apart — fix the spacing.

### Step 3: Independent Practice — Your Own Ocean Animal Flipbook (20 min)

Challenge: *"Now make your OWN flipbook using any Kenyan ocean animal. Minimum 10 frames."*

Give a code-style starter (frame plan template) they can fill in:

```text
MY FLIPBOOK — Animal: ___________  Action: ___________
Total frames: ___   (10–16 recommended)

Key poses (draw these FIRST):
  Frame 1  (start): ____________________________________
  Frame __ (middle): ____________________________________
  Frame __ (end):   ____________________________________

Inbetweens: fill the frames between key poses so the
motion is SMOOTH — small, even steps.

Check before flipping:
  [ ] Background/anchor object stays in the SAME place
  [ ] Each frame moves only a little from the last
  [ ] Body part that trails (tail/fin) bends opposite to motion
```

Suggest animals by motion type (from the ocean animals reference): jellyfish bell pulsing, seahorse tail curling, octopus arm waving, turtle flipper sweep. Remind them: pick a SIMPLE shape so you can redraw it 12 times.

**Low-resource note:** no sticky notes? Use the corner of a notebook — draw in the bottom corner of 12 pages and flip the pages.

### Step 4: Sharing & Feedback (9 min)

Partners flip their books for each other. Use a simple critique frame (from the project's feedback module):
- **One thing that worked** (e.g., "the tail wag looked real")
- **One question** (e.g., "what if you added 2 more frames at the end?")
- **One connection to history** (e.g., "yours is like Gertie because the character has personality")

Celebrate that every learner just did what animators have done for 150+ years.

## Assessment

| Criteria | BE (Below Expectation) | AE (Approaching) | ME (Meets) | EE (Exceeds) |
|----------|------------------------|-------------------|------------|--------------|
| History knowledge | Names 0–1 milestones; cannot place any on a timeline | Names 2 milestones; vague on dates/order | Names 3+ milestones and orders them correctly; explains persistence of vision | Names 3+ milestones, orders them, AND links a modern technique to its historical root |
| Flipbook creation | Fewer than 8 frames; motion does not read; anchor object moves | 8–9 frames; motion is jerky but visible | 10–12 frames; smooth motion; anchor stays fixed; trailing part bends correctly | 12+ frames; smooth, has personality/secondary motion; shows deliberate spacing choices |
| Connection to tradition | No link made between own work and history | States that flipbooks are old | Explains how their flipbook uses the same principle as 1868/2024 animation | Explains the link AND names which historical animator/technique inspired a specific choice |

## Extended Activity

**Homework — "History meets conservation" mini-research:** Choose ONE moment from today's timeline and find one fun fact to share next class (e.g., "Studio Ghibli uses OpenToonz — the same free tool we can use"). Bonus: find out one thing KWS does to protect the reef animal you drew, and add a single "conservation frame" to the end of your flipbook (e.g., a small text frame: "Protect Watamu reef — home of Samaki Dart"). This bridges to the next lesson on workflow/file management where students will start saving these projects properly.

## Differentiation

| For learners who need more support | For advanced learners |
|-------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Provide pre-drawn key poses (frames 1, 6, 12) they only need to inbetween; reduce to 8 frames; let them trace the clownfish outline | Require 16–24 frames; add a SECOND action (fish darts up, then a tentacle reacts with delay = follow-through); challenge them to animate at "12fps on purpose" like Spider-Verse and explain why it feels different |
