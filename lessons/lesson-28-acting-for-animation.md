## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
1. Explain the "see → think → act" chain and why skipping the "think" step makes characters look robotic.
2. Perform and record a mirror exercise acting out three different emotions (happy, scared, curious) as a green sea turtle character, then translate key observations into animation choices.
3. Animate a green sea turtle character showing a clear emotion through body posture, eye movement, and timing — not just facial expression.

## Key Inquiry Question

> A green sea turtle (*Kasa*) glides slowly through the reef — but what
> happens when Kasa sees a plastic bag drifting toward her? How does her
> body, her eyes, and her timing change to show that she is scared? How do
> we ACT that performance into our animation?

## Local Context: Kenyan Ocean Animal

**Animal:** Green Sea Turtle (*Kasa*)  
**Habitat:** Seagrass beds in Watamu Marine National Park, Diani-Chale Marine Reserve, and Malindi Marine National Park. Green sea turtles graze on seagrass meadows — they are the "gardeners" of the reef, keeping seagrass healthy by trimming it.  
**Why this animal?** The green sea turtle is perfect for teaching acting because its SLOW, GRACEFUL movement makes every emotion visible. A fast character (like a clownfish) zips by before you can read its emotion. But Kasa moves slowly — every flipper stroke, every head turn, every pause is readable. When Kasa is calm, the movement is smooth and steady. When Kasa is scared, the same body suddenly moves erratically — flippers tuck in, head withdraws, the whole body flinches. The contrast between calm-turtle and scared-turtle is the entire lesson: **the same character shows different emotions through HOW it moves, not just its face.** Turtles also have expressive eyes and a face that can show curiosity (head tilting up) or fear (head pulling back into the shell).  
**Conservation note:** Green sea turtles are classified as **Endangered** by the IUCN. Their main threats in Kenyan waters are plastic pollution (turtles mistake plastic bags for jellyfish and eat them), fishing nets (bycatch), and habitat loss from coastal development. The Kenya Wildlife Service (KWS) runs a nesting protection program at Watamu — rangers monitor beaches during nesting season (roughly March-July) and protect eggs from predators and poachers. Local organizations like Local Ocean Conservation (LOC) in Watamu rescue injured turtles and release them back to the ocean. The animation learners create today — showing a turtle reacting to plastic — can become a conservation message that real people see and learn from.

## Lesson Development

### Introduction (10 min)

Start with a performance. The teacher (or a brave volunteer) acts out
walking across the classroom in THREE different ways — without saying
what the emotions are:
1. **Happy:** chest out, bouncy steps, big arm movements, head up
2. **Sad:** shoulders hunched, shuffling steps, small movements, head down
3. **Scared:** shoulders raised, quick small steps, leaning back, arms
   close to body

Ask the class: *"What emotion was I showing each time? How could you
tell — I didn't say anything!"*

The answer: **Your body told the story. Your posture, your speed, your
eye direction — that's where emotion lives.**

Now show a 30-second video of a green sea turtle swimming calmly through
a reef. Then show (or describe) a turtle suddenly flinching and tucking
its head when something unexpected appears.

Tell learners: **Today, we're going to learn acting for animation. Not
drawing. Not coding. ACTING. Because the #1 skill in animation isn't
drawing or software — it's performance. Characters need to PERFORM, not
just move.**

Introduce the key idea from the module: *Every great animator is a
performer. You have to FEEL what the character feels to show it on
screen. Before animating a scene, ACT IT OUT. Be silly. Record yourself.
Watch yourself. Then animate what you see.*

### Step 1: Concept Introduction — Acting Principles for Animators (15 min)

Teach the three core acting concepts that make characters feel alive.
Write these on the board:

**1. The "See → Think → Act" Chain**

Characters don't just move — they THINK, then move. Show the thinking:
- **See:** Eyes lead (eyes move first, then head, then body)
- **Think:** A tiny pause (2-4 frames) where the character processes
  what they saw
- **Decide:** A micro-expression (head pull-back, eye widen) showing
  the decision
- **Act:** The body moves based on the decision

```
SEE (eyes dart)  →  THINK (2-4 frame pause)  →  DECIDE (expression)  →  ACT (body moves)
```

**This chain makes characters feel ALIVE. Skip it and they feel like
robots.**

**2. Body Language = Emotion**

Give learners the printed Emotion → Movement reference table:

| Emotion | Posture | Speed | Eyes | Hands/Fins |
|---------|---------|-------|------|------------|
| Happy | Open, upright | Bouncy, varied | Wide, bright | Open, gesturing |
| Sad | Closed, hunched | Slow, shuffling | Down, half-closed | Close to body, limp |
| Scared | Back, small | Fast, erratic | Wide, darting | Close, shaking |
| Curious | Leaning forward | Slow, careful | Wide, searching | Reaching, pointing |
| Angry | Forward, tense | Fast, sharp | Narrow, focused | Fists, sharp |

**3. Timing = Emotion**

How FAST a character moves tells you how they feel:
- **Fast movements:** Urgent, excited, panicked, angry
- **Slow movements:** Sad, tired, careful, thoughtful
- **Sudden stops:** Surprised, shocked, caught
- **Hesitant starts:** Nervous, unsure, reluctant
- **Varied speed:** Natural, alive (constant speed = robotic)

Connect to Kasa the turtle: *A calm turtle moves at a steady, slow pace.
A scared turtle might freeze completely (sudden stop), then dart
erratically (fast, erratic). The SAME turtle shows completely different
emotions just by changing timing and posture.*

### Step 2: Guided Practice — The Mirror Exercise (20 min)

This is the #1 acting exercise for animators, adapted for the classroom.

**Setup:** Each learner (or pair) needs a phone camera or a mirror. If
cameras are limited, learners work in pairs: one acts, one records, then
swap.

**The Exercise:**

Tell learners: *"You are Kasa the green sea turtle. You're gliding through
the seagrass meadow at Watamu. Act out these three scenes. Be the turtle.
Use your whole body. You will look silly — that's fine. Every professional
animator does this."*

**Scene 1 — Curious Kasa (5 min):**
- You're swimming peacefully. You see something interesting on the reef.
- Act it: eyes look first (SEE), pause briefly (THINK), then your head
  tilts toward it (DECIDE), then you swim slowly toward it (ACT).
- Record it. Watch it back. Notice: when did your eyes move? When did your
  body start moving? Was there a pause?

**Scene 2 — Scared Kasa (5 min):**
- You're swimming peacefully. Suddenly you see a plastic bag drifting
  toward you (it looks like a jellyfish — your food — but something is
  wrong).
- Act it: eyes dart to the bag (SEE), freeze for 2 seconds (THINK —
  "what IS that?"), eyes go wide (DECIDE — fear), flippers tuck in and
  you flinch backward (ACT — scared).
- Record it. Watch it back. Notice: how did your posture change? Did you
  lean back? Did your movements get faster and jerkier?

**Scene 3 — Relieved Kasa (5 min):**
- You flinched back from the plastic bag. But then you realize it drifted
  away. You're safe.
- Act it: eyes follow the bag drifting away (SEE), a slow blink and
  slight head dip (THINK — processing), shoulders/fins relax (DECIDE —
  relief), then you slowly resume swimming (ACT).
- Record it. Watch it back. Notice: how did the tension leave your body?
  Did your speed change from fast (scared) back to slow (calm)?

**After all three scenes:** Have learners watch their recordings at
normal speed, then at 0.25x speed (on the phone's video player). At
slow speed, they can see:
- The exact frame where their eyes moved before their head
- The pause between seeing and reacting
- The moment their posture changed from tense to relaxed

Tell them: **This is what you're going to animate. Not a turtle moving.
A turtle FEELING.**

### Step 3: Independent Practice — Animate Kasa's Emotion (20 min)

Now learners translate their mirror exercise into an animation. They use
Scratch (or another tool if they prefer) to animate Kasa the green sea
turtle showing the "curious → scared → relieved" sequence.

**Scratch Setup:**

1. Choose or draw a turtle sprite. Name it "Kasa." Create 3 costumes:
   - Costume 1: "calm" — normal posture, head level, eyes normal
   - Costume 2: "curious" — head tilted slightly up, eyes wider
   - Costume 3: "scared" — head pulled back, eyes very wide, flippers
     tucked (make the sprite slightly smaller to show withdrawal)

2. Add a "plastic bag" sprite (a white blob shape) that drifts in from
   the right side.

3. Set the backdrop to an underwater seagrass scene.

**The Animation Code — Curious → Scared → Relieved:**

```scratch
when [green flag] clicked
  // SCENE 1: CURIOUS KASA (calm swimming, then notices something)
  switch costume to [calm v]
  go to x: (-150) y: (0)
  // Steady, calm swimming — slow glides = relaxed
  glide (2) secs to x: (-50) y: (20)
  glide (2) secs to x: (0) y: (0)
  
  // SEE: eyes notice the plastic bag
  // (Eyes lead! Head tilts toward the bag first)
  switch costume to [curious v]
  wait (0.5) secs          // THINK pause — 12 frames at 24fps
  
  // DECIDE: curiosity → swim closer slowly
  glide (1.5) secs to x: (50) y: (10)
  wait (0.3) secs          // another small think pause
  
  // SCENE 2: SCARED KASA (realizes it's not food — it's plastic!)
  // The plastic bag drifts closer
  broadcast [bag-approaches v]
  
  // SEE + THINK: eyes go wide, freeze
  switch costume to [scared v]
  wait (0.8) secs          // FREEZE — sudden stop = shock
  
  // ACT: flinch backward (fast, erratic = scared)
  glide (0.3) secs to x: (20) y: (40)    // jerk up
  glide (0.2) secs to x: (0) y: (20)     // jerk back
  glide (0.3) secs to x: (-30) y: (10)   // retreat
  
  // Hold the scared pose
  wait (1) secs
  
  // SCENE 3: RELIEVED KASA (bag drifts away)
  // Watch the bag leave — eyes follow it
  broadcast [bag-leaves v]
  wait (1.5) secs          // watching it drift away
  
  // Slow blink + relax
  switch costume to [calm v]
  wait (0.5) secs          // the relief moment — silence speaks
  
  // Slowly resume calm swimming
  glide (3) secs to x: (-200) y: (0)    // slow, steady = calm again
  say [Bahari ni nyumbani kwangu. / The ocean is my home.] for (3) seconds
```

**Plastic bag sprite code:**

```scratch
when I receive [bag-approaches v]
  show
  go to x: (200) y: (15)
  glide (2) secs to x: (80) y: (15)    // drifts toward Kasa

when I receive [bag-leaves v]
  glide (3) secs to x: (250) y: (-20)  // drifts away and down
  hide
```

**Key acting choices learners should notice in their own animation:**
1. **The THINK pause** (0.5-0.8 seconds of stillness) before each reaction
   — this is where the "acting" lives. Without it, Kasa looks robotic.
2. **Eyes lead the body** — the costume switches to "curious" (eyes wide)
   BEFORE Kasa swims closer. The eyes tell the audience what's about to
   happen.
3. **Timing change = emotion change** — calm swimming uses 2-second
   glides (slow); scared flinching uses 0.2-0.3 second glides (fast,
   erratic). The speed IS the emotion.

Learners who finish early can add:
- Sound effects: a gentle water sound during calm swimming, a sudden
  "whoosh" during the flinch, silence during relief
- A second turtle in the background that also reacts
- Text narration appearing at key moments ("Kasa sees something...")
- A conservation message at the end: *"Plastic bags look like jellyfish
  to turtles. Kasa was lucky. Many aren't. Reduce plastic use to protect
  Kasa and her friends."*

### Step 4: Sharing & Critique (10 min)

Learners present their animation to a partner or the class. The audience
watches and tries to identify:
1. **Can you see the "see → think → act" chain?** (Did Kasa's eyes move
   before the body? Was there a pause?)
2. **Can you read the emotion?** (Was "scared" clearly different from
   "curious"?)
3. **Did the timing change?** (Calm = slow, scared = fast?)

Use the critique framework:
- **What's working:** *"The freeze before the flinch really sells the
  fear — great think pause!"*
- **One question:** *"What if the scared flinch was even faster? Right
  now it feels a little smooth for panic."*

**Pro tip to share with the class:** *"Your character doesn't need to
show the emotion — they need to TRY to hide it. A turtle pretending not
to be scared (swimming calmly but with slightly wider eyes) is more
interesting than a turtle openly panicking. Try it in your next animation."*

Celebrate every performance. Remind learners: **Every animator does the
mirror exercise. Every animator looks silly doing it. That's how it works.
The silliness IS the craft.**

## Assessment

| Criteria | BE (Below Expectation) | AE (Approaching) | ME (Meets) | EE (Exceeds) |
|----------|------------------------|-------------------|------------|--------------|
| Mirror exercise performance | Did not act out the scenes or did not record | Acted out scenes but without clear emotion differentiation | Acted and recorded all 3 emotions with clear body language differences (posture, speed, eyes) | Acted with nuanced performance; identified specific frames where eyes led body and where posture changed; used recording to inform animation |
| "See → Think → Act" chain in animation | No think pause; character reacts instantly (robotic) | Has a pause but eyes don't lead; body and eyes move simultaneously | Clear see-think-act chain: eyes move/costume changes first, 2-4 frame pause, then body reacts | Chain is visible AND timing varies by emotion (calm = slow pauses, scared = fast freeze); the audience can read the thought process |
| Emotion through movement (not just face) | Emotion shown only through speech bubbles or labels | Some body change but emotion unclear without reading text | Emotion is readable through posture + timing + eye direction without any text | Multiple emotions are distinct and believable; timing change is the primary tool; animation could serve as a conservation message on its own |

## Extended Activity

**Homework / Follow-up: Kasa's Conservation Story**

Create a 30-60 second animation that tells a complete conservation story
using acting principles:

1. **Kasa is happy** — grazing on seagrass at Watamu, smooth calm swimming
2. **Kasa is curious** — sees a plastic bag, approaches it slowly (eyes
   lead, head tilts)
3. **Kasa is scared** — realizes it's plastic, not food, flinches back
4. **Kasa is sad** — sees other plastic on the reef, slow, hunched posture
5. **Kasa is relieved/hopeful** — a human diver appears and removes the
   plastic; Kasa watches with wide, curious eyes, then slowly resumes
   grazing

This animation can be shared with the school, on social media, or with
Local Ocean Conservation (LOC) in Watamu as a real conservation message
created by young Kenyan animators. **The acting IS the message — when
audiences FEEL Kasa's fear and relief, they understand why plastic
pollution matters more than any statistics could show.**

This is the final lesson in the animation assistant module series. The
acting skills learned here connect back to every previous lesson —
collaboration (acting out scenes together), low-resource animation (you
can act and animate on any hardware), and FAQ (Pweza can now be animated
with real emotion, not just static answers).

## Differentiation

| For learners who need more support | For advanced learners |
|-------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Simplify to ONE emotion (scared only). Provide a pre-made turtle sprite with 2 costumes (calm + scared). Give them a step-by-step checklist: (1) Kasa swims calmly, (2) plastic bag appears, (3) Kasa freezes for 1 second, (4) Kasa moves backward fast. Focus on getting the think-pause right — that's the most important acting concept. | Challenge them to use Krita or Blender instead of Scratch for a more professional result. Add a second character (a diver or another turtle) and create a dialogue scene using the 70/30 eye contact rule (eyes look at the other character 70% of the time, look away 30% while thinking). Add micro-expressions: brow furrow before the scared reaction, slow blink during relief. Challenge: show an emotion the character TRIES to hide (Kasa pretends to be calm but is actually scared — only the eyes reveal it). |
