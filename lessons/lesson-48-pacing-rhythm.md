## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
1. Distinguish between **timing** (frame-level: how many frames per action) and **pacing** (sequence-level: the rhythm of the whole scene).
2. Apply the **Golden Rule of Contrast** (slow ↔ fast) and the **Emotional Wave** (build → climax → decelerate → resolve) to plan a scene's pacing.
3. Create a speed map and shot-by-shot timing plan for a multi-shot animation using a Kenyan ocean animal.

## Key Inquiry Question

> How do we make a scene about **Papa Shinga the Whale Shark (papa shinga)**
> gliding through Diani Reef feel calm and majestic — and then make it feel
> exciting when a speedboat comes too close?

## Local Context: Kenyan Ocean Animal

**Animal:** Whale Shark — *Papa Shinga* (Swahili)  
**Habitat:** Open ocean near Diani-Chale Marine Reserve (Kwale County) and Watamu Marine National Park. Whale sharks visit Kenya's coast from October to February to feed on plankton.  
**Why this animal?** The whale shark is the **perfect pacing teacher**. It is the largest fish in the ocean — up to 18 meters long — and it moves SLOWLY, gliding gracefully as it filters plankton. This natural slowness teaches **slow pacing** (tension, majesty, importance). But when a speedboat approaches too fast, the whale shark can startled and dive rapidly — a sudden **fast pacing** moment. The contrast between the whale shark's calm gliding and a sudden threat is the perfect case study for the Golden Rule of Contrast: **nothing feels fast without slow, nothing feels slow without fast.**  
**Conservation note:** Whale sharks are **endangered** (IUCN Red List). Their main threats in Kenyan waters are boat strikes (from tourist boats and fishing vessels) and fishing. KWS runs a whale shark tagging program and has published tourism guidelines: boats must keep a minimum distance, approach slowly, and limit viewing time. Responsible whale shark tourism at Diani brings income to local communities while protecting the animals. The contrast between respectful slow-approach tourism and dangerous fast-approach boats is literally a pacing lesson that saves lives.

## Lesson Development

### Introduction (10 min)

**Rhythm exercise (3 min):** Clap a slow, steady beat with the class — one clap every 2 seconds. After 8 claps, suddenly clap FAST for 4 claps (2 per second), then go back to slow.

Ask: "How did the fast claps feel after the slow ones?" *(More energetic, surprising, exciting.)* "How would the fast claps have felt if we'd been clapping fast the WHOLE time?" *(Exhausting, boring — they stop feeling fast.)*

Explain: **This is PACING.** Pacing is to animation what rhythm is to music. You can have perfect individual notes (timing) but if the song has no groove (pacing), it feels wrong.

**Show the whale shark video (4 min):** Play 30 seconds of a whale shark gliding slowly through blue water at Diani Reef. Ask learners to notice:
- How slow and smooth the movement is
- How the size of the animal makes it feel important, heavy, calm
- How the rhythm is SLOW — and that slowness IS the feeling

Then ask: "Now imagine a speedboat suddenly races toward the whale shark. How should the PACING of the scene change?" *(The shots should get shorter, faster cuts, building tension.)*

**Introduce Papa Shinga:** "Today we use the whale shark — *papa shinga* — to learn pacing. The whale shark's natural slowness teaches us that SLOW sections are BOLD — they say 'pay attention, this matters.' And when a threat appears, the CONTRAST makes the fast part feel explosive."

### Step 1: Concept Introduction — The 3 Speeds & The Golden Rule (15 min)

**The 3 Speeds of Animation (draw on board):**

| Speed | Shot Length | Use For | Feeling |
|-------|------------|---------|---------|
| **Fast** | 0.5-1 sec | Chase, fights, comedy, climaxes | Urgency, excitement, chaos |
| **Medium** | 2-4 sec | Normal scenes, walking, dialogue | Comfortable, natural |
| **Slow** | 4-6 sec+ | Emotional moments, tension, dramatic reveals | Heavy, important, tense |

**The Golden Rule of Contrast:**

> **Nothing feels fast without slow. Nothing feels slow without fast.**

- A film that's fast the whole time → exhausting
- A film that's slow the whole time → boring
- The magic is in the CONTRAST:
  - Slow → **FAST** → Slow = the fast part feels explosive
  - Fast → **SLOW** → Fast = the slow part feels heavy and important

**The Emotional Wave (draw on board):**

```
                    ┌─────────┐
                   ╱           ╲
          ╱       ╱   CLIMAX    ╲       ╲
        ╱  RISE  ╱   (FAST!)     ╲  COOL  ╲
      ╱         ╱                 ╲  DOWN  ╲
    ╱ BUILD    ╱   (MEDIUM→FAST)   ╲ (SLOW) ╲___ RESOLVE
   (MEDIUM)                              (MEDIUM)

Speed: Slow ──── Medium ──── FAST ──── Slow ──── Medium
        (setup)   (build)    (climax)  (aftermath) (resolve)
```

1. **Build up** (medium): establish the scene, introduce the character
2. **Rising action** (medium getting faster): things start happening
3. **Climax** (fast): the big moment — the hit, the jump, the reveal
4. **Deceleration** (slow): the aftermath — the reaction, the realization
5. **Resolution** (medium): settle into the new normal

**This wave works in a 10-second animation AND a 3-minute film. Scale it to your length.**

**The Boredom Threshold:**
- Most shots feel boring after 4-5 seconds if NOTHING happens
- If a shot is longer than 4 seconds, something must CHANGE within it
- If truly nothing changes (deliberate stillness), keep it SHORT (2-3 seconds max for young audiences)

### Step 2: Guided Practice — Speed Map for Papa Shinga (15 min)

Give each learner a **Speed Map Template**:

```
SPEED MAP: Papa Shinga's Close Call
====================================

Shot 1: _____ sec — [purpose]     Speed: SLOW / MEDIUM / FAST
Shot 2: _____ sec — [purpose]     Speed: SLOW / MEDIUM / FAST
Shot 3: _____ sec — [purpose]     Speed: SLOW / MEDIUM / FAST
Shot 4: _____ sec — [purpose]     Speed: SLOW / MEDIUM / FAST
Shot 5: _____ sec — [purpose]     Speed: SLOW / MEDIUM / FAST

Speed Map:
Slow ──── Medium ──── FAST ──── Slow ──── Medium
(setup)   (build)    (climax)  (aftermath) (resolve)

Total time: _____ seconds
```

**Walk through creating the speed map together for "Papa Shinga's Close Call":**

**The story:** A whale shark glides peacefully through Diani Reef. A speedboat approaches too fast. The whale shark startled and dives deep. The boat passes. The whale shark resurfaces, safe.

**Fill in the speed map as a class:**

```
SPEED MAP: Papa Shinga's Close Call
====================================

Shot 1: 5 sec — Papa Shinga gliding through blue water, plankton drifting    Speed: SLOW
Shot 2: 3 sec — Speedboat appears in the distance, getting closer            Speed: MEDIUM
Shot 3: 1 sec — Boat roars past CLOSE — engine loud, water churns            Speed: FAST
Shot 4: 1 sec — Papa Shinga startled, begins rapid dive                      Speed: FAST
Shot 5: 4 sec — Deep underwater, quiet, whale shark descends slowly          Speed: SLOW
Shot 6: 3 sec — Whale shark resurfaces, glides on, boat is gone              Speed: MEDIUM

Speed Map:
SLOW ──── MEDIUM ──── FAST ──── SLOW ──── MEDIUM
(setup)   (build)     (climax)  (aftermath) (resolve)

Total time: 17 seconds
```

**Discuss the contrast:**
- Shot 1 is SLOW (5 sec) → this makes Shot 3 feel FAST by contrast
- Shots 3-4 are FAST (1 sec each) → this makes Shot 5 feel HEAVY by contrast
- The wave: slow → medium → FAST → slow → medium

**Pacing technique to highlight — The Acceleration Build:**
Shots get progressively shorter to build energy:
- Shot 1: 5 seconds (establishing — slow)
- Shot 2: 3 seconds (action begins — medium)
- Shot 3-4: 1 second each (climax — rapid cuts)
- Shot 5: 4 seconds (aftermath — long, slow, heavy)
- Shot 6: 3 seconds (resolution — return to normal)

"The audience's heart rate follows the shot length. Long shots = calm. Short shots = excitement."

### Step 3: Independent Practice — Prototype the Pacing in Scratch (20 min)

**Challenge:** Build a pacing prototype in Scratch. You don't need beautiful art — use simple shapes. The goal is to FEEL the pacing, not to make polished animation.

**Scratch pacing prototype code:**

This code creates the 6-shot sequence with the correct timing. Each "shot" is a background change + a simple sprite action + a timed wait.

```scratch
when green flag clicked

// Shot 1: SLOW — Papa Shinga gliding (5 seconds)
switch backdrop to [blue-ocean v]
say [Papa Shinga glides through Diani Reef...] for (1) seconds
glide (5) secs to x: (150) y: (0)
wait (1) seconds

// Shot 2: MEDIUM — Boat appears (3 seconds)
switch backdrop to [ocean-with-boat-far v]
say [A speedboat appears in the distance...] for (2) seconds
wait (1) seconds

// Shot 3: FAST — Boat roars past (1 second)
switch backdrop to [ocean-with-boat-close v]
start sound [engine-roar v]
wait (1) seconds

// Shot 4: FAST — Whale shark startled, dives (1 second)
say [!! PAPA SHINGA IS STARTLED !!] for (0.5) seconds
glide (0.5) secs to x: (0) y: (-100)
wait (0.5) seconds

// Shot 5: SLOW — Deep underwater, quiet (4 seconds)
switch backdrop to [deep-blue v]
say [Deep below... quiet... safe... for now] for (3) seconds
wait (1) seconds

// Shot 6: MEDIUM — Resurfaces, glides on (3 seconds)
switch backdrop to [blue-ocean v]
glide (2) secs to x: (0) y: (0)
say [Papa Shinga returns to the surface. The boat is gone.] for (2) seconds
wait (1) seconds

stop [all v]
```

**What learners do:**
1. Type in the code above
2. Create 4 simple backdrops (solid colors are fine: light blue, blue with a small boat shape, blue with a big boat shape, dark blue for deep water)
3. Use a simple sprite as Papa Shinga (a large oval is fine — it's a PROTOTYPE)
4. Add the `engine-roar` sound (or any loud sound from the Scratch library)
5. **Press the green flag and FEEL the pacing**

**After testing, learners adjust:**
- Does Shot 1 feel slow enough? (If not, make it 6 seconds)
- Does Shot 3 feel fast enough? (If not, make it 0.5 seconds)
- Does the contrast work? (Does the fast part feel explosive after the slow start?)
- Is Shot 5 long enough to feel the "aftermath"? (The deceleration is where the emotion lives)

**Key teaching moment:** "If your animation feels boring, it's probably not the animation — it's the PACING. Try cutting 30% of the shots and see if it improves. Or add a SLOW section before a fast one to create contrast."

### Step 4: Sharing & Feedback (10 min)

Learners pair up and play each other's pacing prototypes. Use this feedback framework:

**For each prototype, the partner answers:**
1. **Where did you feel the speed change?** (Did the contrast work?)
2. **Which shot felt too long?** (Boredom threshold check)
3. **Which shot felt too short?** (Did you need more time to process?)
4. **Did the emotional wave work?** (Build → climax → decelerate → resolve?)

**Class discussion:**
- "Whose prototype had the best contrast? What made it work?"
- "Did anyone try a technique we didn't discuss? (e.g., The Hold, The Pregnant Pause)"
- "How would you change the pacing if this were a COMEDY instead of a tense scene?"

**Reinforce the pro tip:**
> "The hold after the action is where the EMOTION lives. Don't cut away too
> soon. Let the audience feel the moment. After the boat roars past, that
> SLOW shot of Papa Shinga descending into the deep — THAT is where we feel
> the whale shark's fear and relief. Without the slow hold, the fast part is
> just chaos."

| Understanding timing vs pacing | Cannot distinguish timing from pacing | Knows they are different but cannot explain how | Clearly explains: timing = frame-level, pacing = sequence-level rhythm | Explains the difference with examples AND can identify both in existing animations |

## Extended Activity

**Homework / follow-up project:** Turn the pacing prototype into a **complete 15-20 second animation** of "Papa Shinga's Close Call" with:
- Hand-drawn or painted backgrounds (Diani Reef, deep ocean, speedboat)
- A proper whale shark character (not just an oval)
- Sound design: calm water sounds → engine roar → silence underwater → calm water returns
- The pacing MUST follow the speed map from this lesson

**Conservation connection:** Add a **title card** at the end: "Whale sharks (papa shinga) are endangered. Boat strikes are a major threat. KWS requires boats to keep distance and approach slowly at Diani-Chale Marine Reserve. Respect the gentle giant." Share your animation with the class or on social media with the hashtag #ProtectPapaShinga.

**Technique challenge for advanced learners:** Try using **The Hold** technique — after the boat passes (Shot 3), hold on a completely still shot of empty water for 2 seconds before showing Papa Shinga diving. This "pregnant pause" creates tension: "Where did the whale shark go? Is it OK?" The audience feels the absence before the relief.
