## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
1. Identify at least two age-appropriate animation contests or challenges and explain their submission requirements.
2. Plan an animation contest entry using a storyboard, timeline, and submission checklist working backward from a deadline.
3. Demonstrate resilience by articulating how to handle contest results — both winning and not winning — with a growth mindset.

## Key Inquiry Question

> A humpback whale (*nyangumi*) spends months building strength before its
> spectacular breach — it anticipates, prepares, and then commits fully. How can
> we apply that same preparation and commitment when entering an animation contest?

## Local Context: Kenyan Ocean Animal

**Animal:** Humpback Whale (*Nyangumi*)  
**Habitat:** Migratory — humpback whales pass through Kenya's Indian Ocean waters from July to September each year, traveling from Antarctic feeding grounds to breeding grounds near the equator. They can be seen off Watamu, Diani, and Shimoni.  
**Why this animal?** The humpback whale's breach is the ultimate "performance" in the ocean. Before breaching, the whale dives deep, builds speed, and commits fully to the jump — this is **anticipation** (an animation principle) and **preparation** (a contest skill). The breach itself is the "submission" — the moment the world sees your work. Some breaches are huge and perfect; some are smaller. But every breach is a commitment. Entering a contest is the same: you prepare, you commit, you submit, and the result is less important than the fact that you did it.  
**Conservation note:** Humpback whales are classified as "Least Concern" and are recovering thanks to the international whaling ban, but they still face threats from ship collisions and ocean noise pollution along migration routes. KWS monitors whale migration seasons along the Kenyan coast and works with shipping companies to reduce collision risks. The recovery of humpback whales is one of conservation's great success stories — proof that sustained effort pays off, just like persistence in animation contests.

## Lesson Development

### Introduction (5-10 min)

Show the 2-3 minute video of a humpback whale (*nyangumi*) breaching off the Kenyan coast. Ask learners:

- *"What happens BEFORE the whale breaks the surface?"* (Answer: it dives deep, builds speed, anticipates)
- *"How long do you think the whale prepared for that 2-second breach?"* (Answer: months of feeding, days of travel, seconds of dive)
- *"What would happen if the whale only half-committed?"* (Answer: it wouldn't clear the water)

Connect to the lesson: **Entering an animation contest is like a whale breach.** You prepare for weeks, you commit fully, and you submit. The contest itself is the moment the world sees your work. Whether you win or not, the breach — the submission — is the achievement. Today we learn how to find the right contest, prepare an entry, and handle the results.

### Step 1: Concept Introduction — Contests, Constraints & Preparation (15 min)

**Why enter contests? (Discuss with class):**
1. **Deadlines force completion** — the #1 reason animations go unfinished is no deadline
2. **Constraints spark creativity** — "30 seconds maximum" or "theme: ocean" forces creative problem-solving
3. **Recognition builds confidence** — even an honorable mention validates months of work
4. **Feedback from judges** — many contests provide free professional review
5. **Portfolio material** — contest entries show you can work to a brief

**Contest catalog (display and discuss):**

| Contest | Ages | What | When | Cost |
|---------|------|------|------|------|
| Scratch Design Studio | 10-13 | Themed Scratch projects, community voting | Ongoing | Free |
| One Earth Young Filmmakers | 10+ | Environmental animation (45s-8min) | Annual (Jan) | Free |
| 11 Second Club | 13+ | Monthly: animate to their audio clip (11 sec) | Monthly | Free |
| 48-Hour Film Project | 14+ | Make a film in 48 hours (team or solo) | Varies | Varies |
| Local school art show | All | School-level animation showcase | Varies | Free |

**The 6-step contest preparation process:**

```
CONTEST ENTRY ROADMAP:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════
Step 1: CHOOSE the right contest
        → Match age, skill level, theme. Start small!
Step 2: READ THE RULES TWICE
        → Length limit, format (MP4 H.264), resolution,
          deadline, theme. Off-theme = disqualified.
Step 3: PLAN your animation
        → Storyboard first! Short, focused, polished.
Step 4: ANIMATE & POLISH
        → Pre-flight checklist, add sound, clean up.
Step 5: PREPARE submission package
        → Animation file + title + synopsis (50-100
          words) + filmmaker bio + consent forms.
Step 6: SUBMIT & LET GO
        → Submit 24 hours early. Don't refresh results.
          Start your NEXT project while waiting.
═══════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**Key rule:** Submit 24 hours early. Don't trust last-minute uploads. Internet in Kenya can be unpredictable — plan for delays.

### Step 2: Guided Practice — Build a Contest Timeline (15 min)

Walk through creating a contest timeline together as a class.

**Scenario:** We're entering the One Earth Young Filmmakers Contest (environmental theme, 45 seconds to 8 minutes, free, annual deadline in January). We have 4 weeks. Our animation will feature a humpback whale (*nyangumi*) migrating past the Kenyan coast — an environmental story about ocean noise pollution.

**Build the timeline on the board (learners copy onto their calendar printouts):**

```
WORKING BACKWARD FROM A DEADLINE:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  Deadline: January 31 (submit by Jan 30 — 24 hrs early!)

  Week 1 (Jan 1-7):  STORYBOARD & PLAN
    - Draw 6-8 storyboard panels
    - Write a 50-word synopsis
    - Decide: Scratch or Krita? 60 seconds? What sound?

  Week 2 (Jan 8-14): ANIMATE — Part 1
    - Build the humpback whale character (simple shape)
    - Animate the swim cycle (arcs, slow-in/slow-out)
    - Rough in the background (ocean, horizon)

  Week 3 (Jan 15-21): ANIMATE — Part 2 & SOUND
    - Add the breach scene (anticimation → breach → splash)
    - Record or find sound effects (splash, whale call)
    - Add narration or title cards with conservation message

  Week 4 (Jan 22-28): POLISH & RENDER
    - Watch 3 times. Fix what bothers you.
    - Export as MP4 H.264, 1280x720
    - Write filmmaker bio: "My name is [username], age [X],
      from Kenya. I use Scratch/Krita. I've been animating
      for [N] months."
    - Get parent consent form signed (REQUIRED for minors)

  Jan 29:      Final review — watch one more time
  Jan 30:      SUBMIT (24 hours early!)
  Jan 31:      Official deadline (we're already done)
  Feb 1+:      Start NEXT project while waiting for results
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**Storyboard mini-exercise (5 min):** Have learners sketch 4 quick panels on their storyboard template:
1. Whale swimming underwater (establishing shot)
2. Whale diving deeper (anticipation)
3. Whale breaching the surface (climax)
4. Whale splashing back, conservation message card (resolution)

### Step 3: Independent Practice — Plan Your Own Entry (20 min)

Learners choose a contest and create a full entry plan.

**Task: Complete the Contest Entry Planning Sheet**

Each learner (or pair) fills out:

```
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
CONTEST ENTRY PLANNING SHEET
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. MY CONTEST: ___________________________
   (Scratch Design Studio / One Earth / 11 Second Club /
    School art show / other)

2. MY ANIMAL CHARACTER: ___________________
   (Must be a Kenyan ocean animal: nyangumi/whale, kasa/
    turtle, pomboo/dolphin, pweza/octopus, papa shinga/
    whale shark, clownfish, etc.)

3. MY STORY (1-2 sentences):
   _______________________________________________
   _______________________________________________

4. LENGTH: ______ seconds (check contest rules!)

5. FORMAT: MP4 H.264 / Scratch project / GIF

6. MY TIMELINE (fill in dates):
   - Storyboard done by: ______
   - Animation done by: ______
   - Sound done by: ______
   - Polish done by: ______
   - SUBMIT by: ______ (24 hrs before deadline!)

7. MY SYNOPSIS (50-100 words):
   _______________________________________________
   _______________________________________________
   _______________________________________________

8. MY BIO (2-3 sentences):
   _______________________________________________
   _______________________________________________
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**Scratch contest entry — whale breach starter code:**

For learners using Scratch, here is a starter for a humpback whale breach animation they can build on:

```scratch
When [green flag] clicked
  // Scene 1: Swimming underwater
  switch backdrop to [underwater]
  go to x: -150, y: -50
  say [Swimming north along the Kenyan coast...] for 2 seconds
  glide 3 secs to x: 0, y: -50  // slow steady swim

  // Scene 2: Anticipation — diving deeper
  say [Time to breach!] for 1 second
  glide 1 sec to x: 0, y: -150  // dive down (anticipation!)
  wait 0.5 seconds

  // Scene 3: The breach — fast upward arc
  switch backdrop to [ocean-surface]
  glide 0.8 secs to x: 0, y: 100  // fast breach (arc!)
  // Splash effect
  start sound [splash]
  create clone of [splash-particles]

  // Scene 4: Fall back + conservation message
  glide 1.2 secs to x: 0, y: -50  // fall back down
  switch backdrop to [conservation-card]
  say [Humpback whales face threats from ocean noise.
       Support KWS marine monitoring programs!] for 5 seconds
```

> **Teacher note:** This is a starting point. Learners should add their own
> costumes, timing adjustments, and sound. The conservation message connects
> the contest entry to a real Kenyan environmental issue.

---

### Step 4: Sharing & Feedback (10 min)

Learners present their Contest Entry Planning Sheet to a partner or small group. Each presentation is 2 minutes:

- Which contest did they choose and why?
- What's their story and which Kenyan ocean animal did they pick?
- What's their timeline — can they realistically finish?

Partners give feedback using the **Two Stars and a Wish** method:
- **Star 1:** Something strong about the plan (e.g., "Great choice of animal — the whale breach is visually dramatic")
- **Star 2:** Another strength (e.g., "Your timeline gives enough time for sound, which many people forget")
- **Wish:** One suggestion (e.g., "I wish your synopsis mentioned the conservation message — that could make it stand out")

**Closing discussion (whole class):**

*"What happens if you don't win?"*

Share the truth: **Finishing IS winning.** Most people never finish an animation. The fact that you planned, animated, and submitted puts you ahead of 90% of people who start. Watch the winners. What did they do that you didn't? Learn from it. Enter the NEXT one. Persistence beats talent.

Connect to the whale: *"A humpback whale doesn't breach perfectly every time. Some breaches are small. Some are huge. But the whale keeps breaching, season after season, year after year. That's persistence."*

## Assessment

| Criteria | BE (Below Expectation) | AE (Approaching) | ME (Meets) | EE (Exceeds) |
|----------|------------------------|-------------------|------------|--------------|
| Contest knowledge & selection | Cannot name any contests or selects one inappropriate for their age/skill | Names 1-2 contests but unclear on requirements; chooses a contest with minimal reasoning | Selects an age-appropriate contest and explains requirements (length, format, deadline, theme) | Selects the best-fit contest with detailed reasoning, compares 2+ options, and justifies the choice |
| Entry planning (timeline + storyboard) | Incomplete or no timeline; no storyboard | Has a timeline but unrealistic (e.g., animating everything in one day); rough storyboard | Has a realistic 4-week timeline, 4+ panel storyboard, synopsis, and bio that meet contest requirements | Has a detailed timeline with buffer time, 6+ panel storyboard, polished synopsis, bio, AND a backup plan if something goes wrong |
| Resilience & growth mindset | Focuses only on winning; discouraged by idea of not winning | Understands finishing is valuable but struggles to articulate how to handle not winning | Articulates how to handle both winning and not winning with a growth mindset (learn from winners, enter next one) | Articulates growth mindset AND identifies specific lessons to learn from winners, with a plan to enter the next contest |

## Extended Activity

**Homework: The Real Submission**

Choose ONE contest from today's lesson. Commit to actually entering it within the next month. Steps:

1. Create your Scratch account (if you don't have one) — for Scratch Design Studio
2. Go to the contest website and read the FULL rules
3. Save the deadline in your phone or write it on a calendar at home
4. Start your storyboard this week
5. Tell a parent/guardian about your plan (especially if consent forms are needed)

**Conservation connection:** Add a 5-second conservation message card at the end of your contest animation. Use a real fact about your chosen Kenyan ocean animal from the KWS website (kws.go.ke) or Local Ocean Conservation (localocean.co). Animation that educates is animation that matters — and judges love entries with a purpose.

## Differentiation

| For learners who need more support | For advanced learners |
|-------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Assign them the Scratch Design Studio (simplest, no formal submission package needed) and provide a pre-made storyboard template with 4 panels already labeled | Challenge them to plan for the 11 Second Club (requires syncing animation to an audio clip — harder) and write a full 100-word synopsis |
| Reduce the timeline to 2 weeks and focus on a 30-second Scratch animation only (no sound editing required) | Have them plan a 48-Hour Film Project entry as a team exercise — assign roles (director, animator, sound, editor) |
| Provide a partially filled planning sheet with the contest and animal already chosen | Ask them to research 3 local Kenyan or African film festivals (e.g., Zanzibar International Film Festival, Lola Kenya Screen) and report back to the class |
