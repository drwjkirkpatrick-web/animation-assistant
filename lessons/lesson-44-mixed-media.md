## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
1. **Describe** at least 3 mixed media animation approaches (collage, photo+drawing, material/sand animation).
2. **Create** a 10-second mixed media animation using a Kenyan ocean animal as the subject, combining at least two different visual materials or techniques.
3. **Value** experimentation and "happy accidents" as legitimate artistic approaches in animation.

## Key Inquiry Question

> What happens when you cut up photos of **Kenya's coral reef** and animate them with hand-drawn fish swimming through them — and why does breaking the rules make animation more personal?

## Local Context: Kenyan Ocean Animal

**Animal:** Coral Reef Ecosystem — **Mwamba wa Makorali** in Swahili  
**Habitat:** Watamu Marine National Park, Malindi Marine Park, Mombasa Marine Park, Diani-Chale Marine Reserve. Kenya's reefs host 600+ species of fish and 110+ species of stony coral.  
**Why this ecosystem?** Coral reefs are naturally mixed media — they combine hard coral structures, soft corals swaying in current, colorful fish darting between, and light filtering through water. This makes them the perfect subject for mixed media animation: cut out coral photos as the background, draw animated fish on top, add real sand or shells as foreground elements. The reef IS a collage of different textures and materials.  
**Conservation note:** Kenya's coral reefs are threatened by bleaching (from rising ocean temperatures), overfishing, and anchor damage from boats. KWS runs reef restoration projects — growing coral fragments and transplanting them onto damaged reefs. Learners can connect their mixed media art to real conservation.

## Lesson Development

### Introduction (10 min)

**Hook:** Show two images side by side:
1. A "standard" digital animation (flat, clean, digital)
2. A mixed media animation (Terry Gilliam / Monty Python style — cut-out photos moving)

Ask: *"Which one feels more unique? Which one could only be made by ONE person?"*

Explain: *Mixed media animation combines different materials — photos, drawings, real objects, textures. It can't be replicated because YOUR cut-outs are one-of-a-kind. Today we'll break the rules and make something that only YOU could make.*

Show photos of Kenya's coral reef. Explain: *We'll use the reef as our subject — cut out coral photos, draw fish on top, add real sand. The reef is naturally mixed media, so it's perfect.*

### Step 1: Mixed Media Approaches (15 min)

Teach 3 approaches that work with phone + basic materials:

**Approach 1: Collage Animation (ages 10+)**
1. Collect images: magazine cut-outs, printed reef photos, colored paper, fabric scraps
2. Cut out shapes: coral, fish, seaweed, rocks
3. Set up phone on a stand pointing down at a flat surface
4. Open Stop Motion Studio
5. Move cut-outs slightly between each frame
6. Capture and play back

**Approach 2: Photo + Drawing (ages 12+)**
1. Take or find a photo of a Kenyan reef or beach
2. Import as background layer in Krita
3. Draw animated characters (fish, turtles, octopus) ON TOP of the photo
4. The photo is "real," the character is "drawn" — the contrast is the style

**Approach 3: Material/Sand Animation (ages 10+)**
1. Get a flat surface (tray, lightbox, table)
2. Get your material: sand, salt, beads, small shells from Kenyan beaches
3. Set up phone overhead
4. Shape and reshape the material frame by frame
5. Capture with Stop Motion Studio

### Step 2: Guided Demo — Reef Collage (20 min)

Walk through creating a collage animation step by step:

**Setup:**
1. Print or cut out: coral photos, blue paper (ocean), sand-colored paper (seabed)
2. Arrange on flat surface: blue paper as background, sand paper at bottom, coral cut-outs standing up
3. Position phone overhead using a stand

**Animation — 10 frames (about 10 seconds at 1fps):**

```
Frame 1: Coral reef static, no fish
Frame 2: A clownfish cut-out appears at left edge
Frame 3: Move clownfish slightly right
Frame 4: Move clownfish more — it's swimming
Frame 5: Add a sea turtle cut-out at right edge
Frame 6: Move clownfish and turtle toward center
Frame 7: They meet in the middle — turtle looks at fish
Frame 8: Add a plastic bag cut-out drifting in from top (CONFLICT!)
Frame 9: Turtle mistakes bag for jellyfish — moves toward it
Frame 10: Clownfish "warns" turtle — moves between turtle and bag
```

**In Stop Motion Studio:**
```scratch
// While Stop Motion Studio handles capture,
// you can also do collage in Scratch:
// 1. Upload reef photo as backdrop
// 2. Upload fish cut-out photo as sprite
// 3. Animate the sprite moving across the photo background

when [green flag] clicked
switch backdrop to [reef-photo]           ← Real photo of Kenyan reef
go to x: (-200) y: (0)
show
glide (2) secs to x: (200) y: (0)         ← Fish "swims" across real photo
say [Watch out for plastic!] for (2) seconds
```

### Step 3: Independent Practice (20 min)

**Challenge:** Create a 10-second mixed media animation about a Kenyan ocean animal.

**Options:**
- **Collage:** Cut out reef photos and animate fish moving through them
- **Photo + Drawing:** Take a photo of the school yard, draw an octopus crawling across it
- **Material:** Use sand to shape a sea turtle, then reshape it swimming away frame by frame
- **Typography:** Animate the word "BAHARI" (ocean in Swahili) — each letter bounces, stretches, and morphs into a sea creature shape

**Rules:**
1. Must use at least 2 different materials/techniques
2. Must feature a Kenyan ocean animal or ecosystem
3. Must include one conservation element (plastic bag threat, protection message, etc.)
4. Have fun — this is about experimentation, not perfection!

### Step 4: Sharing & Feedback (15 min)

Learners present their mixed media pieces. Discuss:
- *"What materials did you combine? Why?"*
- *"What surprised you about this process?"*
- *"What 'happy accident' happened that you kept?"*

Celebrate the uniqueness: *"No two pieces look the same — that's the power of mixed media. Your cut-outs, your drawings, your sand — they're one of a kind."*

## Assessment

| Criteria | BE (Below Expectation) | AE (Approaching) | ME (Meets) | EE (Exceeds) |
|----------|------------------------|-------------------|------------|--------------|
| Media Mixing | Uses only one material/technique | Uses 2 materials but they don't interact | Combines 2+ materials intentionally for contrast | Combines 3+ materials with clear artistic purpose |
| Creativity | Copies the demo exactly | Some variation but similar to demo | Original concept with unique material choices | Surprising, personal, can't be replicated |
| Conservation Element | No conservation message | Mentioned but not visual | Clear visual conservation element | Conservation message is integral to the artwork |

## Extended Activity

**Reef Restoration Animation:** Create a 30-second mixed media piece showing reef restoration: start with a damaged reef (broken coral, grey colors), then show new coral being planted (colorful cut-outs appearing), fish returning, and the reef coming back to life. Use real sand and shells from a Kenyan beach for the seabed, photos of coral for the reef, and hand-drawn fish. Submit to a school art exhibition with a conservation message.

---

## Differentiation

| For learners who need more support | For advanced learners |
|-------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Provide pre-cut reef photos and fish shapes | Try 2D + 3D integration: render a 3D coral in Blender, composite with 2D cut-out fish |
| Focus on collage only (simplest approach) | Try rotoscope + mixed media: trace video of a real ocean, add collage elements on top |
| Use only 5 frames instead of 10 | Try experimental approaches: draw without looking, animate to random sounds, one color + one shape |
| Work in pairs (one moves, one captures) | Use Krita layers: photo background + hand-drawn middle layer + cut-out foreground = 3-layer depth |
