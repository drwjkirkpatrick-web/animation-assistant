## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
1. Distinguish between **AI as a tool** (ethical), **AI as a shortcut** (dangerous), and **AI as a replacement** (harmful) in animation.
2. Apply the **Learning Test** ("If I didn't have AI, could I make this?") to evaluate their own use of AI in animation projects.
3. Value honest disclosure of AI assistance and commit to learning animation fundamentals before using AI to speed up workflow.

## Key Inquiry Question

> If I use AI to generate a picture of **Pomboo the Dolphin (pomboo)**
> jumping out of the water at Kisite-Mpunguti, am I still an animator —
> or am I just typing?

## Local Context: Kenyan Ocean Animal

**Animal:** Bottlenose Dolphin — *Pomboo* (Swahili)  
**Habitat:** Kisite-Mpunguti Marine Reserve (Kwale County), Watamu Marine National Park. Dolphins are frequently seen in pods playing and jumping near the Kenyan south coast.  
**Why this animal?** A dolphin jumping out of the water is one of the most iconic animation subjects — it involves anticipation (the dive down), arcs (the jump trajectory), and follow-through (the splash). It's also a perfect case study for AI ethics: AI can generate a *picture* of a dolphin jump instantly, but only a trained animator understands *why* the jump looks good (arcs, timing, weight). This lesson uses the dolphin to show the difference between **generating** and **animating**.  
**Conservation note:** Dolphins in Kenyan waters face threats from fishing bycatch, pollution, and disruptive boat traffic from unregulated dolphin-watching tourism. KWS regulates dolphin-watching at Kisite-Mpunguti Marine Reserve — boats must keep distance, avoid chasing pods, and limit viewing time. Respectful dolphin tourism protects the pods while still allowing communities to earn income from visitors.

## Lesson Development

### Introduction (8 min)

Show two images side by side (on projector):
- **Image A:** An AI-generated image of a dolphin jumping out of the water (teacher generates this beforehand, or use a pre-prepared example)
- **Image B:** A photo of a real bottlenose dolphin jumping at Kisite-Mpunguti Marine Reserve

Ask the class:
- "Which one took 5 seconds to make?" *(Image A — the AI image)*
- "Which one do you understand better — why the dolphin looks the way it does?" *(Lead them to see: the photo shows REAL physics — real arcs, real splash, real weight. The AI image looks good but we don't know WHY it looks good.)*

Explain: **Today we're talking about AI and animation. AI can make images and even animation. But the question isn't "is AI good or bad?" — it's "WHEN and HOW should I use it?"**

Introduce Pomboo the Dolphin: "We're going to use Kenya's bottlenose dolphin — *pomboo* — as our case study. By the end of this lesson, you'll know exactly when AI helps you animate and when it hurts you."

### Step 1: Concept Introduction — The Three Stances (15 min)

Draw this diagram on the whiteboard:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              THE THREE STANCES ON AI                     │
│                                                          │
│  Stance 1: AI as TOOL (Good)      Stance 2: AI as        │
│  ┌──────────────────────┐        SHORTCUT (Dangerous)   │
│  │ AI helps you animate  │        ┌──────────────────┐  │
│  │ better. YOU do the    │        │ AI does the       │  │
│  │ work; AI assists.     │        │ creative work.    │  │
│  │                       │        │ You skip learning.│  │
│  │ Examples:             │        │                   │  │
│  │ - Reference images    │        │ Examples:         │  │
│  │ - Color palettes      │        │ - AI walk cycles  │  │
│  │ - Texture generation  │        │ - AI character    │  │
│  │ - Audio cleanup       │        │   design          │  │
│  │ - Ideation (ideas)    │        │ - AI inbetweens   │  │
│  └──────────────────────┘        │ - AI story        │  │
│                                   └──────────────────┘  │
│                                                          │
│         Stance 3: AI as REPLACEMENT (Harmful)            │
│         ┌──────────────────────────────────────┐        │
│         │ AI replaces the animator entirely.    │        │
│         │ "Generate a 30-sec animated short."   │        │
│         │ The human did nothing creative.       │        │
│         │ → Not learning, not creating,         │        │
│         │   not animation (it's prompting)      │        │
│         └──────────────────────────────────────┘        │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Teach The Learning Test (5 min):**

The single most important question: **"If I didn't have AI, could I make this?"**

| Answer | What it means | Action |
|--------|--------------|--------|
| **YES** (with more time) | You're using AI as a tool. ✅ | Continue — you have the skill, AI speeds you up |
| **NO** (I don't know how) | You're using AI as a crutch. ❌ | Stop. Learn the skill first. |
| **NO** (but I'm learning) | You're in transition. ⚠️ | Use AI for reference only. Do the animation yourself. |

**Teach The Portfolio Test (3 min):**
- Recruiters CAN tell if animation is AI-generated (inconsistent frames, weird interpolation, "too smooth")
- If you can't animate live in an interview, you can't claim the skill
- **Rule:** Never put AI-generated animation in a portfolio as if you animated it. If you used AI as a reference tool, SAY SO. **Honesty > impressiveness.**

**The industry reality (2 min):**
- Studios use AI for backgrounds, crowd animation, inbetweening (assisted, not replaced)
- The Animation Guild (animators' union) fights for protections: AI assists, not replaces
- **Character animation, acting, and creative direction remain human** — these require emotion and intention AI can't provide
- Learn the FUNDAMENTALS (12 Principles, timing, acting) — AI can't replace understanding

### Step 2: Guided Practice — The AI Ethics Decision Card (15 min)

Give each learner an **AI Ethics Decision Card**:

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         AI ETHICS DECISION CARD                     │
│                                                     │
│  Situation: ____________________________            │
│                                                     │
│  Step 1: The Learning Test                          │
│  "If I didn't have AI, could I make this?"          │
│  [ ] YES (with more time) → AI as tool ✅           │
│  [ ] NO (I don't know how) → AI as crutch ❌        │
│  [ ] NO (but I'm learning) → Reference only ⚠️      │
│                                                     │
│  Step 2: What AI CAN help with here:               │
│  __________________________________________        │
│                                                     │
│  Step 3: What AI should NOT do here:               │
│  __________________________________________        │
│                                                     │
│  Step 4: My decision:                              │
│  [ ] Use AI as a tool (ethical)                    │
│  [ ] Don't use AI — learn the skill first          │
│  [ ] Use AI for reference only, animate myself     │
│                                                     │
│  Step 5: Disclosure note (if I use AI):            │
│  "I used AI to ______, but I animated ______       │
│   myself."                                          │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Walk through 3 scenarios together as a class. Fill out the card for each:**

**Scenario 1:** "I want to animate Pomboo the dolphin jumping, but I don't know what a dolphin jump looks like. Should I ask AI to generate a dolphin jump animation?"

- **Learning Test:** NO — I don't know how to animate a jump yet. → AI as crutch ❌
- **AI CAN help:** Generate a *reference image* of a dolphin mid-jump (not an animation)
- **AI should NOT do:** Generate the animation frames for me
- **Decision:** Use AI for reference image only. Study the arc. Animate the frames myself.

**Scenario 2:** "I animated Pomboo jumping myself (12 frames). Now I want AI to suggest a color palette for the ocean background. Is that OK?"

- **Learning Test:** YES — I can pick colors myself; AI just speeds it up. → AI as tool ✅
- **AI CAN help:** Suggest a blue/teal palette for the Indian Ocean
- **AI should NOT do:** Nothing wrong here — this is ethical use
- **Decision:** Use AI as a tool. Disclosure: "AI suggested the color palette; I applied it myself."

**Scenario 3:** "I need to make a 30-second animation for a school project due tomorrow. I want to ask AI to generate the whole thing. Is that OK?"

- **Learning Test:** NO — I can't animate a 30-second film yet. → AI as replacement ❌
- **AI CAN help:** Nothing — this is asking AI to replace me entirely
- **AI should NOT do:** Generate the entire animation
- **Decision:** Don't use AI to generate. Scope down to what I CAN animate (5-10 seconds). Check school AI policy. Animate it myself.

### Step 3: Independent Practice — Animate vs Generate (20 min)

**Challenge:** Animate **Pomboo the Dolphin jumping out of the water** — BY HAND, without AI generating any frames.

**Step-by-step in Scratch:**

1. Create a dolphin sprite (draw it or use Scratch's built-in dolphin)
2. Create 8 costumes showing the jump arc:

```markdown
Frame 1: Pomboo underwater, swimming up (anticipation)
Frame 2: Pomboo approaching surface
Frame 3: Pomboo breaking the surface (launch)
Frame 4: Pomboo rising (arc going up)
Frame 5: Pomboo at apex (top of arc — hang)
Frame 6: Pomboo descending (arc coming down)
Frame 7: Pomboo hitting water (impact — splash)
Frame 8: Pomboo underwater again (recovery)
```

3. Program the jump animation:

```scratch
when green flag clicked
set [y v] to (-100)
forever
  wait (1) seconds
  switch costume to [pomboo-1 v]
  wait (0.1) seconds
  change y by (20)
  switch costume to [pomboo-2 v]
  wait (0.1) seconds
  change y by (30)
  switch costume to [pomboo-3 v]
  wait (0.1) seconds
  change y by (25)
  switch costume to [pomboo-4 v]
  wait (0.15) seconds
  change y by (10)
  switch costume to [pomboo-5 v]
  wait (0.2) seconds
  change y by (-10)
  switch costume to [pomboo-6 v]
  wait (0.15) seconds
  change y by (-25)
  switch costume to [pomboo-7 v]
  wait (0.1) seconds
  change y by (-30)
  switch costume to [pomboo-8 v]
  wait (0.1) seconds
  change y by (-20)
  set [y v] to (-100)
end
```

**Notice the timing:** The apex (frame 5) has a **longer wait** (0.2s) — this is the "hang" at the top of the jump. This is pacing AND timing. The descent is faster (0.1s waits) — gravity pulls Pomboo down faster than she rose.

**After animating, learners write their disclosure note:**

> "I animated Pomboo the dolphin jumping using 8 hand-drawn frames in
> Scratch. I watched a reference video of a real dolphin jumping at
> Kisite-Mpunguti to understand the arc. I did NOT use AI to generate
> any frames. I animated every frame myself."

**Compare:** If the teacher showed an AI-generated dolphin jump at the start, learners now compare: "My animation has INTENTION — I chose where the apex hang is, I chose the splash frame. The AI didn't make artistic choices — I did."

### Step 4: Sharing & Discussion (7 min)

Learners share their dolphin animations and their disclosure notes. Class discussion:

- "How did it feel to animate the jump yourself instead of having AI do it?"
- "What did you LEARN by animating each frame that you wouldn't have learned from generating?"
- "When would AI have been helpful in this project?" *(Reference image, color palette, audio cleanup)*

**Reinforce the ethical guidelines:**

**DO:**
- ✅ Use AI for reference and inspiration
- ✅ Use AI to clean up audio, generate textures, suggest palettes
- ✅ Be HONEST about what AI helped with
- ✅ Learn the skill yourself before using AI to speed it up
- ✅ Treat AI as an assistant, not a replacement

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Generate full animations and claim you made them
- ❌ Use AI to skip learning fundamentals
- ❌ Put AI-generated work in your portfolio without disclosure
- ❌ Let AI make creative decisions for you
- ❌ Use AI to copy another artist's style and pass it off as original

**The real question:** "Are you making animation, or are you making prompts? They're different skills. The joy of animation is in the MAKING. If AI does the making, where's your joy?"

## Extended Activity

**Homework / follow-up project:** Create a **"My AI Ethics Statement"** — a 1-paragraph personal commitment to how you will use AI in animation. Include:
- 2 ways you WILL use AI ethically (reference, palettes, textures, audio cleanup)
- 2 ways you will NOT use AI (generating full animation, skipping fundamentals)
- Your disclosure template: "I used AI to ____. I animated ____ myself."

**Conservation connection:** Create a **dolphin protection poster** (animated or static) for Kisite-Mpunguti Marine Reserve. Use AI to generate a *reference image* of the marine reserve landscape, then draw and animate the dolphin yourself. Disclose the AI use on the poster. Message: "Watch dolphins from a distance. Respect their space. KWS protects pomboo at Kisite-Mpunguti."

**Next lesson connection:** Lesson 48 covers **pacing & rhythm** — now that you can animate a dolphin jump, how do you control the FLOW of the whole scene? When should it be fast, when slow?
