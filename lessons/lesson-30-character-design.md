## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
1. **Identify** the three basic shape languages (circles, squares, triangles) and the personality traits each communicates.
2. **Design** an original character based on a Kenyan Indian Ocean marine animal using shape language, silhouette testing, and a 3-color palette.
3. **Create** a simple model sheet (front + side view) for their character that could be used for animation.

## Key Inquiry Question

> What shape is a **green sea turtle (Kasa)** — and how do the shapes you choose tell the audience who your character is before it even moves?

## Local Context: Kenyan Ocean Animal

**Animal:** Green Sea Turtle (*Chelonia mydas*) — **Kasa** in Swahili  
**Habitat:** Seagrass beds in Watamu Marine National Park and Diani-Chale Marine Reserve. Green turtles nest on Kenya's beaches at Watamu and Tiwi.  
**Why this animal?** The sea turtle's body is a perfect circle (the shell) with triangular flippers and a round head. It demonstrates mixed shape language: circles say "friendly, gentle, approachable" while the triangular flippers say "dynamic, can move fast when needed." This makes the turtle a great teaching example for how shapes combine to create complex characters.  
**Conservation note:** Green sea turtles are classified as Endangered. They face threats from plastic pollution (they eat plastic bags thinking they are jellyfish), fishing net bycatch, and habitat loss from coastal development. KWS and Local Ocean Trust run nesting protection programs at Watamu — volunteers patrol beaches at night to protect nesting females and their eggs.

## Lesson Development

### Introduction (10 min)

**Hook:** Show three silhouettes on the board — a circle, a square, and a triangle. Ask learners:

- *"If these were characters, which one is the hero? Which is the villain? Which is the strong silent type?"*

Show photos of three Kenyan ocean animals:
1. **Clownfish** — round body, round fins → friendly, cute
2. **Moray Eel** — long, angular, triangular head → dangerous, sharp
3. **Green Sea Turtle** — round shell, triangular flippers → gentle but capable

Explain: *Shapes have a secret code. Before your character moves or speaks, the audience already knows who they are — just from the shapes. Today we'll learn this code and design our own ocean characters.*

### Step 1: Shape Language Theory (15 min)

Teach the three shape languages using ocean animal examples:

| Shape | Personality | Ocean Animal Example | Character Use |
|-------|------------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Circle** | Friendly, warm, cute, safe | Clownfish, sea turtle shell, pufferfish | Heroes, sidekicks, gentle characters |
| **Square** | Strong, solid, dependable, stubborn | Whale shark body, crab shell | Leaders, tough characters, protectors |
| **Triangle** | Dynamic, dangerous, sharp, aggressive | Moray eel head, lionfish spines, shark teeth | Villains, predators, fast characters |

**Mixing shapes:** Most interesting characters mix two shapes:
- **Kasa the Turtle** = Circle (shell) + Triangle (flippers) = gentle but can move fast
- **Pweza the Octopus** = Circle (head) + Triangle (tentacles) = clever and dangerous
- **Papa Shinga the Whale Shark** = Square (body) + Circle (mouth) = powerful but gentle

**The silhouette test:** Fill your character with solid black. Can you tell what it is? Can you tell its personality? If yes → good design. If it's a blob → add contrast.

### Step 2: Guided Practice — Designing Kasa (20 min)

Walk through designing a sea turtle character step by step:

**Step 1: Personality first**
```
Name: Kasa
Species: Green Sea Turtle
Traits: Wise, patient, brave, slow but determined
Wants: To clean the reef of plastic
Flaw: Too slow to catch fast prey
```

**Step 2: Dominant shape**
- Wise + patient + brave → Circles dominant (shell = big circle)
- But can move when needed → Triangular flippers for dynamic contrast

**Step 3: Silhouette first — draw a solid black shape**
Draw a big circle (shell), a smaller circle (head), and four triangles (flippers). Fill it all black. Can you tell it's a turtle? Yes!

**Step 4: Add contrast**
- Big shell + small head = wise, older character
- Short flippers = not a fast swimmer
- Round eyes = kind, gentle

**Step 5: Color palette (3 colors max)**
```
Primary:   Olive green (#6B8E23) — shell (camouflage in seagrass)
Secondary: Sandy tan  (#D2B48C) — underside
Accent:    Dark brown  (#5C4033) — shell pattern lines
```

**Step 6: Model sheet**
Draw Kasa from the front and from the side:

```scratch
// In Scratch, create two costumes:
// Costume 1: "kasa-front" — front view (shell visible, head at top, flippers spread)
// Costume 2: "kasa-side" — side view (profile, one front flipper visible, one back flipper)

when [green flag] clicked
switch costume to [kasa-front]
say [I am Kasa. I have lived in this reef for 50 years.] for (3) seconds
switch costume to [kasa-side]
say [I may be slow, but I never give up.] for (3) seconds
```

### Step 3: Independent Practice — Design Your Own Ocean Character (25 min)

**Challenge:** Each learner designs their own character from a Kenyan ocean animal.

Provide the animal list: clownfish, parrotfish, butterflyfish, lionfish, moray eel, octopus, seahorse, jellyfish, dolphin, whale shark, dugong, crab.

**Design checklist:**
- [ ] Wrote personality (3 traits + what they want + one flaw)
- [ ] Chose dominant shape (circle, square, or triangle — or mix)
- [ ] Did silhouette test (filled black — can identify the animal?)
- [ ] Added contrast (big vs small, round vs sharp)
- [ ] Chose 3-color palette
- [ ] Drew front view AND side view
- [ ] Named the character (Swahili name encouraged!)

### Step 4: Sharing & Feedback (10 min)

Learners hold up their designs. The class guesses the personality from the shapes alone:
- *"What personality do you see in this design?"*
- *"Which shape is dominant? What does it tell you?"*
- *"Does the silhouette read clearly?"*

Celebrate creative choices. Connect to conservation: *"Your octopus character has triangular tentacles — that says 'clever and maybe dangerous.' Octopuses ARE clever — they can open jars and change color!"*

## Assessment

| Criteria | BE (Below Expectation) | AE (Approaching) | ME (Meets) | EE (Exceeds) |
|----------|------------------------|-------------------|------------|--------------|
| Shape Language | Cannot identify shapes in character design | Identifies shapes but cannot connect to personality | Uses shapes deliberately to communicate personality | Mixes shapes for complex, multi-dimensional characters |
| Silhouette Test | Silhouette is unidentifiable | Silhouette is somewhat recognizable | Silhouette clearly reads as the animal + personality | Silhouette reads clearly AND has dynamic, interesting pose |
| Model Sheet | Only one view, inconsistent proportions | Two views but proportions differ | Front + side view with consistent proportions | Front + side + expression sheet with full consistency |

## Extended Activity

**Model Sheet Exchange:** Trade character designs with a partner. Try to draw each other's character in a new pose (jumping, swimming, sleeping). If you can't keep the design consistent, the original designer needs to simplify. This teaches why model sheets matter for animation.

**Conservation tie-in:** Add a conservation badge to your character — a symbol on their shell/body showing what threat they fight. Kasa fights plastic pollution. What does your character fight?

---

## Differentiation

| For learners who need more support | For advanced learners |
|-------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Provide pre-drawn shape templates (circles, squares, triangles to trace) | Create a full turnaround (front → 3/4 → side → back) and an expression sheet with 6 emotions |
| Limit to 2 colors instead of 3 | Design two characters that contrast (circle hero vs triangle villain) and show how their shapes interact |
| Focus on silhouette test only (skip color for now) | Consider how the design affects animation: "big shell = hard to squash, long tentacles = lots of follow-through" |
