## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
1. **Develop** a short animation story using the five-step framework: Spark → Character → Setting → Structure → Storyboard.
2. **Create** a 6-panel storyboard with shot descriptions, camera angles, action, principle focus, and approximate duration for each panel.
3. **Implement** the first 3 panels of their storyboard as a working Scratch animation using a Kenyan ocean animal as the main character.

## Key Inquiry Question

> "How do we turn 'Kasa the Green Turtle wants to reach the seagrass meadow' into a plan we can actually animate — panel by panel?"

## Local Context: Kenyan Ocean Animal

**Animal:** Green Sea Turtle (*Kasa*)  
**Habitat:** Seagrass beds and coral reefs of Watamu Marine National Park, Diani-Chale Marine Reserve, and Kisite-Mpunguti Marine Reserve — adults graze on seagrass meadows  
**Why this animal?** Kasa the Green Turtle is a natural story character: patient, wise, and determined. A turtle swimming toward a goal (the seagrass meadow) gives us a clear **want** (the character motivation that drives every story). The journey across the reef provides natural obstacles (a coral wall, a plastic bag that looks like a jellyfish, a strong current) — and the turtle's slow, graceful swimming is perfect for teaching **arcs**, **timing**, and **slow-in/slow-out**. The conservation angle is built into the story itself.  
**Conservation note:** Green sea turtles are classified as **Endangered** by the IUCN. They face threats from plastic pollution (turtles mistake plastic bags for jellyfish and eat them), fishing bycatch, and habitat loss as seagrass meadows decline. KWS protects turtle nesting sites at Watamu — rangers monitor beaches at night during nesting season and relocate eggs to safe enclosures. Local conservation groups like Local Ocean Conservation in Watamu rescue injured turtles and release them back to the sea. *Linda kasa — protect the turtle.*

## Lesson Development

### Introduction (8 min)

**Hook:** Show a 15-second video (or describe from photos) of a green sea turtle gliding across a reef toward a seagrass meadow.

Ask: *"If this turtle were the main character of a story, what does she WANT? What could get in her way?"*

Write learner ideas on the board: "she wants food," "she wants to find a safe place," "a shark could chase her," "she could eat a plastic bag by mistake."

Say: *"Every animation starts with a story — and every story starts with a plan. Today we learn the five steps from idea to storyboard, and then we animate the first part. Plan before you animate."*

Introduce **Kasa the Green Turtle** as today's character.

---

### Step 1: Concept Introduction — The Five-Step Story Framework (15 min)

Teach the five-step framework from Prompt 16, building Kasa's story live on the board:

### Step 1: The Spark (What's the idea?)
Ask the class: *"What if a sea turtle swims toward her favourite seagrass meadow, but something blocks her path?"*

That "what if" is the spark. Every animation starts with one.

### Step 2: The Character (Who is this?)
Build a character profile on the board:

```
CHARACTER CARD
Name:          Kasa
Personality:   Patient, determined, gentle
Visual feature: Large dark shell, big calm eyes, slow flippers
What she WANTS: To reach the seagrass meadow for dinner
```

One personality trait. One visual feature. One clear want. That's all you need.

### Step 3: The Setting (Where are we?)
One sentence: *"A coral reef in Watamu Marine National Park, with a seagrass meadow visible in the distance."*

### Step 4: The Structure (Beginning, Middle, End)
For ages 12-14, use the 6-beat structure:

| Beat | What happens |
|------|-------------|
| 1. Beginning | Kasa is swimming toward the seagrass meadow. She's hungry. |
| 2. First attempt | She swims forward but a strong current pushes her back. |
| 3. Obstacle | A coral wall blocks her path. She can't go through — she must go over. |
| 4. Escalation | She tries to climb over but a plastic bag floats by. She mistakes it for a jellyfish (food!) and swims toward it. |
| 5. Climax | At the last moment she realises it's plastic, not food. She turns away and pushes over the coral wall. |
| 6. Resolution | She reaches the seagrass meadow and grazes peacefully. Safe. |

### Step 5: The Storyboard (Visual plan)
A storyboard is a comic strip of your animation. Each panel = one shot.

Show the class a completed 6-panel storyboard for Kasa's story:

| Panel | Shot description | Camera | Action | Principle focus | Duration |
|-------|-----------------|--------|--------|-----------------|----------|
| 1 | Kasa swimming left-to-right across a colourful reef | Wide | Gentle flipper strokes; seagrass meadow visible far right | Slow in/slow out (smooth swimming) | 4 sec |
| 2 | Current pushes Kasa backward; she flippers harder | Medium | Kasa leans forward, flippers speed up, but slides back | Timing (effort vs. resistance) | 3 sec |
| 3 | Kasa reaches a tall coral wall — stops | Medium close-up | Kasa halts, looks up at the wall, eyes widen | Staging (clear focal point on the wall) | 3 sec |
| 4 | Plastic bag drifts in from the right; Kasa turns toward it | Medium | Kasa's head turns, eyes follow the bag, she starts swimming toward it | Anticipation (head turn before body follows) | 4 sec |
| 5 | Kasa nears the bag — realises it's not food — turns sharply away | Close-up | Kasa's eyes narrow, sharp turn, flippers push hard | Squash & stretch (body bends on the turn) | 3 sec |
| 6 | Kasa glides over the wall and reaches the seagrass; she grazes peacefully | Wide | Kasa descends to the seagrass, begins eating, calm flipper movement | Arcs (smooth descent curve) | 4 sec |

**Total estimated animation: ~21 seconds**

Explain the key rule: *"Don't use the same camera angle twice in a row. Wide → Medium → Close-up → Wide. Variety keeps the audience engaged."*

### Step 2: Guided Practice — Build Your Own Storyboard (20 min)

Learners create their own 6-panel storyboard using the template. They can use Kasa the Green Turtle OR choose a different Kenyan ocean animal from the reference list.

**Animal options for storytellers:**
- **Kasa the Green Turtle** — patient, determined, wants to reach seagrass
- **Samaki Dart the Clownfish** — tiny but brave, darts through danger
- **Pweza the Octopus** — clever, many arms, solves problems
- **Papa Shinga the Whale Shark** — gentle giant, biggest in the ocean
- **Pomboo the Dolphin** — playful, energetic, always jumping

**Storyboard template (each learner fills in 6):**

```
PANEL ___
Sketch: [draw the shot here]
Shot description: ___________________________________
Camera: [Wide / Medium / Close-up]
Action: ____________________________________________
Principle focus: ____________________________________
Duration: ___ seconds
```

Learners sketch each panel (simple stick figures are fine — storyboards are plans, not art) and fill in the metadata.

Walk the room and check:
- Does every panel have a **different camera angle** from the one before it?
- Is there a clear **want** in Panel 1?
- Does something **go wrong** in the middle panels?
- Is there a **resolution** in the final panel?
- Does each panel name an **animation principle**?

**Storyboard planning checklist (display on board):**

```markdown
STORYBOARD CHECKLIST — tick each box before you start animating:
  [ ] Panel 1 establishes the character and what they want
  [ ] No two consecutive panels have the same camera angle
  [ ] Something goes wrong in panels 2-4 (conflict!)
  [ ] The final panel shows a resolution (success or funny failure)
  [ ] Every panel names ONE animation principle to focus on
  [ ] Total duration is written and realistic (10-40 seconds)
```

### Step 3: Independent Practice — Animate the First 3 Panels in Scratch (25 min)

Learners animate **Panels 1, 2, and 3** of their storyboard in Scratch. (Panels 4-6 are homework — see Extended Activity.)

Using Kasa's storyboard as the reference, here is the complete Scratch implementation for Panels 1-3:

```scratch
// ============================================
// KASA THE GREEN TURTLE — Storyboard Panels 1-3
// ============================================

// PANEL 1: Wide shot — Kasa swims across the reef
// Principle: SLOW IN / SLOW OUT (smooth swimming)
when [green flag] clicked
  // Set up the scene
  switch backdrop to [watamu-reef]
  go to x: (-220) y: (-20)
  switch costume to [kasa-swim-1]
  // Slow in/slow out: break the swim into segments
  //   with varying speeds to simulate easing
  glide (1) secs to x: (-120) y: (-10)    // slow start
  glide (0.6) secs to x: (-40) y: (0)     // speed up
  glide (0.8) secs to x: (40) y: (-5)     // cruise
  glide (1) secs to x: (120) y: (0)       // slow down
  // Costume switching = simple swim cycle (2 frames)
  //   This simulates frame-by-frame via costume changes
  forever
    switch costume to [kasa-swim-1]
    wait (0.3) seconds
    switch costume to [kasa-swim-2]
    wait (0.3) seconds
  end

// PANEL 2: Medium shot — current pushes Kasa back
// Principle: TIMING (effort vs. resistance)
//   Use a SEPARATE script triggered by a broadcast
when I receive [panel-2-current]
  stop [other scripts in sprite]
  go to x: (120) y: (0)
  // Kasa tries to go right but current pushes left
  //   Timing: short glides show struggle
  repeat (3)
    glide (0.3) secs to x: (150) y: (0)   // push forward
    glide (0.4) secs to x: (110) y: (0)   // current pushes back
  end
  // She ends up further back than she started — frustration!
  say [This current is strong!] for (2) seconds

// PANEL 3: Medium close-up — coral wall blocks the path
// Principle: STAGING (clear focal point on the wall)
when I receive [panel-3-wall]
  stop [other scripts in sprite]
  go to x: (110) y: (0)
  // Kasa halts and looks up at the wall
  //   Staging: turtle is centered, wall sprite appears above
  say [A wall of coral... I must go over it.] for (3) seconds
  // Small head-tilt up = anticipation of looking up
  turn [clockwise] (5) degrees
  wait (0.5) seconds
  turn [counter-clockwise] (5) degrees

// CORAL WALL sprite (separate sprite)
when I receive [panel-3-wall]
  show
  go to x: (200) y: (60)
  // Staging: wall is large and centered — clear focal point
  set size to (150) %

// DIRECTOR script — controls the panel sequence
when [green flag] clicked
  // Panel 1 runs (above)
  wait (6) seconds
  broadcast [panel-2-current]
  wait (5) seconds
  broadcast [panel-3-wall]
```

> **Teacher prep before lesson:** Create the following in Scratch:
> - **Backdrops:** `watamu-reef` (a simple reef scene — blue background with brown/green coral shapes)
> - **Kasa costumes:** `kasa-swim-1` and `kasa-swim-2` (turtle with flippers in 2 different positions — a 2-frame swim cycle)
> - **Coral wall sprite:** a tall brown/green rectangle (can be very simple)
>
> Learners can also draw these themselves in 10 minutes if you prefer — simple shapes are fine.

Learners follow the code to build Panels 1-3, then **customise** it to match their OWN storyboard (different animal, different obstacles, different principles).

Circulate and ask:
- *"Which panel are you on? Which animation principle is the focus for this panel?"*
- *"Does your code match what you drew in your storyboard? If not, which one is right — the code or the storyboard?"* (Answer: the storyboard is the plan. If the code reveals a better idea, update the storyboard. Plans change — that's okay.)

---

### Step 4: Sharing & Feedback (12 min)

Learners do a **"Storyboard Pitch"**: they show their paper storyboard to a partner and narrate the full 6-panel story (even though only 3 are animated). Then they play their Scratch animation for Panels 1-3.

Partner gives feedback using the **"I notice… I wonder…"** framework:

- *"I notice your Panel 1 has great slow-in/slow-out — the varying glide speeds make Kasa feel heavy and real."*
- *"I wonder what happens in Panel 4? Your storyboard shows the plastic bag — will you animate that next?"*

Celebrate:
- The most **creative obstacle** (what goes wrong for the character)
- The clearest **staging** (one focal point per panel)
- The best **storyboard-to-code match** (the animation faithfully follows the plan)

Close with the core message: *"Plan before you animate. The storyboard is your map. Without it, you get lost. With it, you know exactly what to build next."*

## Assessment

| Criteria | BE (Below Expectation) | AE (Approaching) | ME (Meets) | EE (Exceeds) |
|----------|------------------------|-------------------|------------|--------------|
| **Story structure** | Story has no clear conflict or resolution; character's want is unclear | Story has a beginning and end but the middle (obstacle/conflict) is weak or missing | Story has clear want (Panel 1), obstacle (Panels 2-4), and resolution (Panel 6) with a Kenyan ocean animal as character | Story has all 6 beats with escalating conflict, a clear character arc, AND a conservation message woven in naturally |
| **Storyboard completeness** | Fewer than 4 panels completed; missing camera/action/principle metadata | 4-5 panels completed; some metadata missing or camera angles repeat consecutively | All 6 panels completed with shot description, camera (no repeats), action, principle focus, and duration | All 6 panels completed AND the storyboard demonstrates varied pacing (slow build, fast climax) and clear staging in every panel |
| **Scratch implementation (Panels 1-3)** | Animates 1 panel; no animation principles visible in code | Animates 2 panels; at least 1 principle is visible (e.g., slow-in/slow-out via glide segments) | Animates all 3 panels; code demonstrates 2+ principles; animation follows the storyboard plan | Animates all 3 panels; 3+ principles demonstrated; code is well-organised with broadcasts/panel sequencing; storyboard and code are consistent |

## Extended Activity

**Homework — Finish the Story:** Animate Panels 4, 5, and 6 of your storyboard in Scratch. Add the obstacle (plastic bag for Kasa, or your own obstacle), the climax (the turn-away moment), and the resolution (reaching the goal). Bring the complete 6-panel animation to the next lesson for a class showcase.

**Conservation connection — "Kasa's Real Story":** Research one action that Kenyan conservation groups are taking to protect green sea turtles. Options:
- Local Ocean Conservation (Watamu) — turtle rescue and release
- KWS nesting beach patrols
- Community plastic clean-ups on the coast

Write 3 sentences about what you found and add it as a **final title card** in your Scratch animation: *"Kasa's story has a happy ending. In real life, you can help. [Your conservation fact here]. Linda kasa — protect the turtle."*

## Differentiation

| For learners who need more support | For advanced learners |
|-------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Provide a pre-filled storyboard with the character and setting already chosen — they fill in the obstacles and resolution. Give them the full Scratch code for Panels 1-3 to type in, rather than adapting it. Reduce to a 4-panel story. | Challenge them to create an 8-12 panel storyboard with the full short-film structure (setup, inciting incident, rising action, climax, resolution). Require at least 3 different camera angles and a pacing change (slow build → fast climax → quiet resolution). Have them animate Panels 1-4 in Scratch. |
